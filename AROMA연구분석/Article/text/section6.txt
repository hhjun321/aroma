#6. Conclusion

We present AROMA, a framework that automatically selects ROI modeling and placement policies from dataset complexity statistics (MCI and CCI) via a Meta Policy Generator. This design removes the per-domain handcrafted re-engineering required by prior context-aware synthesis such as CASDA. The principal contribution is this methodological framework for automatic, per-dataset policy selection. It holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces without per-dataset re-engineering. Our downstream findings are reported honestly and conditionally rather than as a uniform performance improvement. Under a training-free copy-paste engine, AROMA's context-aware ROI selection yields a consistent positive direction over random placement on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX +4.96 pp, Severstal +1.06 pp mAP@0.5), though with only n=3 seeds the paired comparison does not reach statistical significance. In contrast, datasets with diminished baseline headroom (MTD −0.25 pp) and homogeneous backgrounds (MVTec Leather −4.91 pp) converge or reverse. Across the roster the AROMA−random gap tracks context complexity. The single-class pair — high-CCI AITeX (0.440) versus low-CCI Kolektor (0.224) — bears this out while also exposing its limits. AITeX, which also retains baseline headroom, carries the positive direction (+4.96 pp), whereas Kolektor's uniform metal surface converges with random (Δ(A−R)=−0.68 pp, n.s.). Kolektor is a null point consistent with the thesis: where baseline headroom vanishes, context complexity becomes irrelevant. We therefore read the gain as jointly conditional on baseline headroom and CCI rather than as either factor alone. This work therefore both delimits where ROI selection cannot help (low-headroom and monotone-background regimes) and indicates where it points positively (heterogeneous surfaces with baseline headroom). Furthermore, it grounds the entire policy pipeline in measurable dataset statistics rather than per-domain handcrafting.

**Principal limitation and future work.** AROMA's core limitation concerns the scope of its compatibility reasoning. The framework reasons about plausible defect placement by matching defect morphology to background surface texture — where on the inspected surface is the defect contextually plausible given local texture patterns? This surface-centric design is well-suited to datasets whose frame content is dominated by the inspected surface itself (textile, steel, leather, magnetic tile). However, it does not reason about the target object or product occupying the frame; it reasons only about background context. This scope limitation becomes acute in object-centric datasets where a discrete manufactured product occupies the frame center against a surrounding background (e.g., MVTec Transistor, Kolektor), because placement plausibility there depends on product geometry and layout, not surface texture alone. In such regimes, AROMA's texture-centric defect placement can propose contextually incoherent locations that respect surface patterns but violate object structure, limiting its real-world applicability. Extending the placement stage with an object (foreground) lens — segmenting the product instance and reasoning about defect placement relative to its geometry — would enable the same data-driven policy machinery to generalize across both surface-centric and object-centric datasets, substantially broadening the applicable domain.

Secondary improvements for statistical power should strengthen the downstream evidence with more seeds and detector sizes to move the observed positive direction toward statistical significance, and identify or construct a low-CCI dataset that retains baseline headroom, enabling genuine isolation of surface heterogeneity from detection difficulty.
