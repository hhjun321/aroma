##4.2. Downstream Detection Performance (AITeX, Kolektor)

Two datasets were selected to evaluate the downstream utility of AROMA under distinct data regimes: AITeX, a single-class tiled-pattern dataset with defect instances confined to one anomaly type; and Kolektor, a small-scale single-class metal-commutator dataset. These datasets represent orthogonal challenges: AITeX tests the policy under morphological homogeneity and tiled spatial structure, whereas Kolektor examines performance in the limited-data regime (36 defect-bearing real training images) where synthetic augmentation is most critical. Downstream detection was assessed via supervised YOLOv8n training under three augmentation conditions (Baseline: no synthetic data; Random: random ROI placement; AROMA: compatibility-aware placement) over three random seeds (1, 2, 42). Results are reported as mAP@0.5 on the shared real test set. Section 4.3 completes the downstream evidence on the three multi-class datasets under the same uniform supervised protocol (Exp4v2); together, the two sections constitute the five-dataset downstream evaluation.

As shown in Tables 6 and 7, the two datasets exhibit contrasting augmentation responses: AITeX demonstrates substantial AROMA advantage, whereas Kolektor converges across methods near ceiling performance.

On AITeX (Table 6), AROMA achieves 0.4683 mean mAP@0.5, outperforming both Baseline (0.2039, +129% relative gain) and Random (0.4187, +12% relative gain). The improvement is robust across seeds: Baseline is highly unstable (std 0.1138, CI95 straddling zero), whereas both augmented methods stabilize performance. AROMA's edge over Random is modest and consistent in direction across seeds, though not statistically significant at n = 3: AROMA's tighter confidence interval and lower variance suggest more uniform placement quality across the tiled defect space (Figure 4.2-1). The single-class, morphologically homogeneous nature of AITeX—combined with its tiled repetitive structure—appears amenable to context-aware placement: AROMA's ability to identify and prioritize compatible background contexts within the tile geometry yields measurable gains.

On Kolektor (Table 7), baseline performance is already near-ceiling (0.974 mAP@0.5); synthetic augmentation provides only marginal gains. Random achieves 0.9938 (+2% relative), and AROMA achieves 0.9870 (−1% relative to Random, +1% absolute vs. Baseline). At this ceiling level, both placement strategies converge (Figure 4.2-2): the 36-image Kolektor training set, combined with high intrinsic dataset quality and balanced defect representation, leaves little headroom for contextual placement to improve detection. The high-variance seed 42 (both Random and AROMA at 0.995 or below) reveals that small-dataset randomness can dominate augmentation effects. AROMA's performance parity with Random—despite its compatibility-aware design—indicates that in near-ceiling, low-headroom regimes (Kolektor; see also MTD in Section 4.3), the placement policy contributes less than baseline dataset characteristics.

The contrast between AITeX and Kolektor validates the conditional nature of AROMA's benefit: meaningful gains emerge when baseline headroom exists (AITeX: low baseline, high improvement potential) and the defect-background compatibility structure is informative (AITeX: diverse tile contexts; Kolektor: already well-covered by random placement in a small, balanced set). No claim of universal improvement is asserted; instead, the results demonstrate that AROMA recovers value in constrained regimes where random placement leaves coverage gaps, while remaining neutral in high-headroom or data-rich scenarios.
