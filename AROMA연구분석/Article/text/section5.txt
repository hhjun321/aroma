#5. Discussion
The principal contribution of AROMA is methodological. The defect–background compatibility model that prior context-aware synthesis (CASDA) constructed by hand is re-derived automatically from patch-level co-occurrence statistics: a symmetric compatibility gate scores placement locations directly from each dataset's own profiling, removing the per-domain handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules that CASDA required. This design advance holds independently of downstream accuracy, and it was applied across five heterogeneous industrial surfaces (textile, metal, steel, machined ceramic, leather) without per-dataset re-engineering. The complexity indices (MCI, CCI) serve as descriptive statistics of each dataset's difficulty; the CCI additionally identifies where context-aware placement can matter (Section 4). Two scope restrictions apply: the gate's individual components (the compatibility threshold and the clean-background gate) are not separately ablated — the reported comparison measures the full placement framework against a naive random baseline (Section 3.3.5) — and no ablation of the ranking weights (0.6/0.4) is attached, as no such run is committed; any such claim is deferred until measured.

Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally. Under the training-free copy-paste engine (see §§4.2–4.3), AROMA's context-aware ROI selection yields a consistent positive direction over random placement on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX +4.96 pp, Severstal +1.06 pp mAP@0.5), though the n=3 paired comparison does not reach statistical significance (paired t, df=2). MVTec Leather presents a near-homogeneous (monotone) background in which defect placement is largely background-agnostic; ROI selection has no informative compatibility signal, and AROMA reverses direction (−4.91 pp). Read across the roster, these outcomes trace a single ordering: the AROMA−random gap tracks the context-complexity index (CCI), running from the high-CCI heterogeneous surface (AITeX, CCI 0.440, +4.96 pp) through the intermediate steel surface (Severstal 0.306, +1.06 pp) to near-zero on the magnetic-tile surface (MTD 0.246, −0.25 pp) and into negative territory on the near-uniform leather surface (CCI 0.200, −4.91 pp). This is the central reading we advance: AROMA's downstream benefit is conditional — realized only where two factors hold jointly, baseline detection headroom and context complexity (CCI) — rather than a uniform improvement. Context-aware selection is effective where the baseline still leaves room to improve and the surface is diverse enough for placement position to matter, and it converges with random placement wherever either condition fails. The two single-class datasets, which fix class granularity at one, bear directly on this reading while also exposing its limits. High-CCI AITeX (0.440) retains substantial baseline headroom (0.204) and carries the positive direction (+4.96 pp). In contrast, low-CCI Kolektor (0.224) is a uniform metal-commutator surface whose exp4v2 baseline turned out near-ceiling (0.974), so AROMA and random converge (Δ(A−R)=−0.68 pp, n.s.). Kolektor is therefore a null point consistent with the thesis: where baseline headroom vanishes, AROMA and random become interchangeable regardless of surface heterogeneity. We treat baseline headroom and CCI as jointly operative factors, both required for a placement gain. Full-image distribution metrics (FID [33], KID [34], LPIPS [35]) are not used as evidence: because both arms composite the same defect pixels, such global metrics are near-identical between arms by construction and measure the global distribution rather than the localized placement effect, so supervised detection (§§4.2–4.3) is treated as the primary evidence.

**Limitations and Future Work.** Three limitations bound the interpretation of these results. First, the AROMA-versus-random comparison rests on only n=3 seeds per dataset; the observed positive direction on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX, Severstal) is consistent but underpowered, and the paired t-tests (df=2) do not reach statistical significance, so the effect should be read as a directional trend rather than a proven improvement. Second, the AITeX baseline is provisional: early stopping on patience left it under-trained (per-seed 0.321/0.197/0.094, std 0.114), so absolute AITeX numbers should be treated cautiously — though the AROMA−random difference is computed within the same training regime and is therefore unaffected by the baseline level. Third, context-matched placement is constrained by clean-background feasibility: measured clean-background coverage of in-matrix context cells is highly uneven across datasets (AITeX 77.8%, MTD 67.2%, MVTec Leather 4.7%), so realistic ROI placement is reliable only on AITeX, whereas full-frame datasets (Severstal) and low-coverage surfaces (Leather) offer diminished opportunity for compatibility-based selection. The third limitation points to a specific extension of the placement mechanism itself, rather than to a data or statistical-power gap. AROMA's compatibility gate reasons entirely about surface texture — whether the defect's morphology matches the surrounding background texture — which is sufficient only because the present roster is dominated by surface-centric surfaces (textile, steel, ceramic, leather) whose frame content is the inspected surface itself. Augmenting the ROI selection stage with an object (foreground) lens — segmenting the inspected product and reasoning about defect placement relative to its geometry, alongside the existing texture-based reasoning — would let the same data-driven policy machinery extend from surface-centric detection to a broader, multi-domain class of object-centric surfaces where a discrete manufactured part occupies the frame against a background.
