#4. Experimental Results and Analysis
This section reports the empirical evaluation of AROMA across the five industrial datasets of §3.1 (AITeX, Kolektor, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) along three complementary axes. Experiment 0 (Section 4.1) evaluates the ROI selection policy through coverage and background-selection statistics, thereby isolating the contribution of the data-driven placement mechanism. Experiments 1 and 2 (Sections 4.2 and 4.3) measure the downstream impact of the augmented data on supervised detection performance (mAP@0.5) — Section 4.2 on the two single-class datasets (AITeX, Kolektor) and Section 4.3 (Exp4v2) on the three multi-class datasets; together they constitute the primary empirical evidence, and their conditional scope is made explicit. Throughout, AROMA is compared against a real-only baseline (Baseline) and, depending on which mechanism is being isolated, one of several distinct random-control arms (Random) — an independently and uniformly re-selected ROI set for the coverage analysis of §4.1 (§3.3.3), the same ROI set with only its background assignment randomized for the background-selection test of §4.1 (§3.3.4), and an independently re-selected ROI set pasted without any placement or background grounding for the downstream detection comparison of §4.3 (§3.3.1). These arms are not interchangeable: each isolates a different component of the placement policy, so a "Random" result in one figure does not carry over to another.

##4.1. ROI Quality Evaluation
The compatibility-driven selection of Section 3.2.4 deliberately concentrates placements on compatible morphology–context combinations rather than spreading them uniformly. To characterize the resulting placement distribution independently of synthesis quality, three coverage statistics (§3.3.3) were computed over AROMA's final placements and an independently, uniformly re-selected Random set drawn from the same candidate pool, at equalized selection size: morphology coverage, context coverage, and rare-pair coverage — the fractions of available morphology clusters, context cells, and rarely co-occurring morphology–context pairs that received at least one placement.
**Figure 4.1-1.** ROI placement coverage (morphology, context, and rare-pair) for AROMA versus an equal-size uniform Random selection from the same candidate pool. Both arms cover all morphology clusters; Random attains equal or higher context coverage by construction, while AROMA's per-pair quota keeps rare-pair coverage at or above Random on AITeX, Severstal, and MTD (MVTec Leather has no rare pairs).

As shown in Figure 4.1-1, both arms cover every morphology cluster, and the uniform Random arm attains equal or higher context coverage on all five datasets (most visibly MTD, 0.85 vs. 0.48) — an expected consequence of the design, since AROMA concentrates placements on compatible cells rather than spreading them. The per-pair coverage quota nevertheless keeps AROMA's rare-pair coverage at or above Random's on AITeX (1.00 vs. 0.87), Severstal (0.99 vs. 0.93), and MTD (1.00 vs. 0.68); Kolektor is the exception (0.60 vs. 1.00), and MVTec Leather's candidate pool contains no rare pairs. Placement breadth is thus traded for compatibility, and the downstream consequence of this trade is measured in Section 4.3.

**Figure 4.1-2.** Qualitative ROI placement comparison. Baseline shows the real defect region; AROMA and Random overlay five identical-size candidate boxes scored by compatibility versus placed uniformly at random.

Figure 4.1-2 illustrates this behavior qualitatively by directly invoking the same compatibility-scan and uniform-random placement functions on one fixed representative image per dataset — a constructed illustration of the placement mechanism itself, distinct from the independently re-selected Random arm of Figure 4.1-1. The compatibility-scored candidates concentrate away from void or texture-degenerate regions (for example, the dark plate edge in Severstal), whereas the uniformly placed candidates disregard local context and land on such regions.

**Figure 4.1-3.** Background-selection compatibility (AROMA vs. Random) across the five datasets: texture-histogram intersection between each ROI's assigned background and the pooled real defect background.

To test the background-selection mechanism independently of the copy-paste engine, AROMA's ROI set is given a symmetric random-background control that keeps the same ROIs and randomizes only the background assignment (§3.3.4) — distinct from the independently re-selected Random arm of Figure 4.1-1. Each ROI's assigned normal background was scored by its texture-histogram intersection with the dataset's pooled real defect background (0–1, higher is more similar), then the two arms were compared with a one-sided Mann-Whitney U test. As shown in Figure 4.1-3, AROMA's assigned backgrounds score higher than Random's on four of the five datasets (AITeX: Δ = +0.071, p = 1.7×10⁻¹¹; Severstal: Δ = +0.032, p = 3.1×10⁻²³; Kolektor: Δ = +0.015, p = 1.2×10⁻⁴; MVTec Leather: Δ = +0.079, p = 2.8×10⁻⁴²), with MTD the exception (Δ = +0.005, not significant, p = 0.25).
