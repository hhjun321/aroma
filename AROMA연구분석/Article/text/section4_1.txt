#4. Experimental Results and Analysis
This section reports the empirical evaluation of AROMA across the five industrial datasets of §3.1 (AITeX, Kolektor, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) along three complementary axes. Experiment 0 (Section 4.1) evaluates the quality of the ROI selection policy through coverage and distributional statistics, thereby isolating the contribution of the data-driven placement mechanism. Experiment 1 (Section 4.2) assesses the perceptual quality of the synthesized images. Experiment 2 (Section 4.4) measures the downstream impact of the augmented data on supervised detection performance (mAP@0.5); this measure constitutes the primary empirical evidence and is reported honestly and conditionally. Throughout, AROMA is compared against a real-only baseline (Baseline) and, depending on which mechanism is being isolated, one of several distinct random-control arms (Random) — an independently and uniformly re-selected ROI set for the coverage analysis of §4.1 (§3.3.3), the same ROI set with only its background assignment randomized for the background-selection test of §4.1 (§3.3.4), and an independently re-selected ROI set pasted without any placement or background grounding for the downstream detection comparison of §4.4 (§3.3.1). These arms are not interchangeable: each isolates a different component of the placement policy, so a "Random" result in one figure does not carry over to another.

##4.1. ROI Quality Evaluation
The objective of the ROI selection policy is to place synthetic defects such that the resulting augmented set spans the morphology–context space as uniformly as possible, with particular emphasis on underrepresented combinations. To quantify this objective independently of synthesis quality, three statistics were computed over AROMA's selection and an independently, uniformly re-selected Random set drawn from the same candidate pool (§3.3.3), at equalized selection size: morphology coverage, context coverage, and rare-pair coverage. These metrics measure the fraction of the available morphology, context, and rare morphology–context cells that received at least one placement.

Figure 7 illustrates this behavior qualitatively by directly invoking the same compatibility-scan and uniform-random placement functions on one fixed representative image per dataset — a constructed illustration of the placement mechanism itself, distinct from the independently re-selected Random arm of Figure 6. The compatibility-scored candidates concentrate away from void or texture-degenerate regions (for example, the dark plate edge in Severstal), whereas the uniformly placed candidates disregard local context and land on such regions.

**Figure 8.** Background-selection compatibility (AROMA vs. Random) across the five datasets: texture-histogram intersection between each ROI's assigned background and the pooled real defect background.

To test the background-selection mechanism independently of the copy-paste engine, AROMA's ROI set is given a symmetric random-background control that keeps the same ROIs and randomizes only the background assignment (§3.3.4) — distinct from the independently re-selected Random arm of Figure 6. Each ROI's assigned normal background was scored by its texture-histogram intersection with the dataset's pooled real defect background (0–1, higher is more similar), then the two arms were compared with a one-sided Mann-Whitney U test. As shown in Figure 8, AROMA's assigned backgrounds score higher than Random's on four of the five datasets (AITeX: Δ = +0.071, p = 1.7×10⁻¹¹; Severstal: Δ = +0.032, p = 3.1×10⁻²³; Kolektor: Δ = +0.015, p = 1.2×10⁻⁴; MVTec Leather: Δ = +0.079, p = 2.8×10⁻⁴²), with MTD the exception (Δ = +0.005, not significant, p = 0.25).
