#1. Introduction
With the progression of smart manufacturing, defect inspection in industrial production has shifted from manual examination to deep learning-based Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) systems [1]. These systems now achieve high reliability across diverse sectors, including steel, semiconductors, and printed circuit boards, and have established themselves as a core technology for quality assurance. In particular, one-class anomaly detection models that learn solely from normal data have attracted considerable attention, because they avoid the prohibitive cost of collecting and annotating exhaustive defect samples [2]. Nevertheless, the performance of these models still depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of the available training data, which limits their effectiveness in data-scarce industrial settings [3].

In real-world manufacturing environments, acquiring sufficient defect samples is inherently difficult, because defects occur infrequently and exhibit highly variable shapes, scales, and textures [4]. This scarcity of minority-class defects produces severe class imbalance, which in turn degrades the generalization capability of detection models [5]. Conventional augmentation techniques, such as geometric transformations and color perturbations, partially expand data diversity. However, they fail to capture the structural properties of defects or their contextual relationship with the surrounding background [6]. More recently, generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion-based methods, have shown promise in synthesizing realistic defects [7, 8]. Despite this promise, they require substantial computational resources and frequently produce samples that are physically inconsistent with the target substrate.

To address these limitations, context-aware synthesis has emerged as an effective strategy that explicitly models the relationship between defect morphology and background surface properties. CASDA (Context-Aware Steel Defect Augmentation) exemplifies this paradigm by constructing a compatibility matrix that encodes defect–background coexistence probabilities and synthesizing contextually consistent samples through a conditional generative pipeline [9]. Such context-aware approaches have demonstrated that respecting the structural relationship between a defect and its substrate yields more discriminative training samples than context-agnostic patch reuse.

Despite this progress, existing context-aware frameworks share a critical scalability bottleneck: their conditioning mechanisms are designed manually for a specific domain. CASDA, for instance, relies on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific morphological rules tuned for steel surfaces. Applying it to a new dataset therefore requires substantial re-engineering of these components. Consequently, the question of how ROI modeling and placement policies can be derived automatically from intrinsic dataset characteristics—rather than crafted by hand for each domain—remains insufficiently explored. A data-driven mechanism that adapts the augmentation policy to the complexity of an arbitrary industrial dataset would substantially improve the generality of context-aware synthesis.

To this end, we propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a framework that overcomes the per-domain engineering burden by learning ROI modeling policies directly from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA first characterizes each dataset through two quantitative descriptors—the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and the Context Complexity Index (CCI)—and a Meta Policy Generator translates these statistics into appropriate clustering and placement policies via distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation on a small subset. Guided by these policies, the pipeline extracts and classifies ROIs, places real defect crops through a data-driven symmetric compatibility gate, and composites them onto normal backgrounds with a training-free copy-paste compositing engine using gradient-domain (seamless)/alpha blending. The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- **A data-driven complexity policy framework.** We introduce Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and Context Complexity Index (CCI) statistics that automatically determine ROI modeling and placement policies via a Meta Policy Generator. This replaces the per-dataset handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules required by prior frameworks like CASDA with data-derived counterparts computed anew for each dataset. The same pipeline applies across heterogeneous datasets without per-domain re-engineering, making this a design/scalability advance independent of downstream accuracy.
- **An auditable policy-selection mechanism.** We design a Meta Policy Generator that selects among candidate clustering and context-partitioning policies through distribution diagnostics, complexity-ranked candidate pruning, and empirical policy evaluation, making the otherwise opaque policy decision reproducible from measured statistics.
- **A characterization of when context-aware ROI selection helps.** We identify the conditions under which context-aware ROI selection improves downstream detection. The effect is conditional on two factors: sufficient baseline headroom and surface heterogeneity (high CCI). On diverse surfaces retaining baseline room, context-aware placement yields consistent gains over random selection. On near-homogeneous or near-ceiling surfaces, AROMA converges with random selection by construction. This characterization frames context-aware placement as conditionally beneficial—realized where both factors hold jointly, absent otherwise—rather than uniformly superior.
- **An honest, CCI-conditional downstream evaluation.** We evaluate AROMA across five heterogeneous industrial datasets—textile (AITeX), metal (Kolektor), steel (Severstal), magnetic tile (MTD), and leather (MVTec)—under a single training-free synthesis engine without cherry-picking. On high-CCI datasets with baseline headroom (AITeX and Severstal), context-aware placement yields consistent gains over random, though n=3 paired comparison does not reach statistical significance. On high-baseline (Kolektor: 0.974) or low-heterogeneity (MVTec Leather) datasets, AROMA converges with random placement, confirming the conditional nature of the benefit. We report null points and confounds explicitly, framing the downstream benefit as jointly conditional on baseline headroom and surface heterogeneity, realized where both hold and absent otherwise.
