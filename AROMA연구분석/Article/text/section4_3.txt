##4.3. Supervised Detection Evaluation (Exp4v2)

Section 4.2 reported the supervised downstream evaluation on the two single-class datasets; this section completes the downstream evidence on the three multi-class datasets under the same uniform supervised protocol (Exp4v2; Section 3.3.2). For completeness, we note that an earlier one-class anomaly-detection experiment (exp4) was discarded during post-hoc auditing: defect-bearing synthetic images had been inadvertently routed into the nominal ("good") training partition, violating the one-class assumption and confounding its AUROC gains. Exp4v2 is free of this confound by design—defects are treated as explicitly labeled foreground objects, and the synthetic data is used as a conventional labeled training set rather than as a perturbation of a nominal manifold.

Exp4v2 employs YOLOv8 detectors under a baseline-then-augment protocol. A model is first trained on the real training partition (Baseline), and each augmented condition then adds synthetic defect images—with their co-saved ROI masks converted to bounding-box labels—to the same training partition under an identical fixed synthesis budget (Section 3.3.5). Ground-truth masks of the held-out real test split are converted to YOLO-format boxes for evaluation, and detection quality is reported as mAP@0.5 over 3 seeds (seeds 1, 2, 42) with paired testing. The evaluation focuses on the three multi-class datasets (Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) under the training-free copy-paste compositing engine of §3.2. Baseline / Random / AROMA differ only in the placement mechanism (Section 3.3.5), so the comparison is a controlled ablation of the full data-driven placement framework.

**Severstal (Table 8, Figure 4.3-1)** shows AROMA +1.06 pp over Random (0.5041 vs. 0.4935 mAP@0.5) from a low baseline (0.496) that retains substantial headroom. Class-wise, AROMA gains are balanced: c1 +1.67 pp, c3 −0.12 pp (neutral), but c4 recovers +2.09 pp vs. Random. Class c2 (underrepresented) remains the weakest across all methods (0.3330 AROMA).

**MTD (Table 9, Figure 4.3-2)** shows augmentation gains from a near-ceiling baseline: Random adds +3.71 pp over Baseline, but AROMA merely matches (0.9440 ≈ Random 0.9465, Δ−0.25 pp). Per-class divergence is notable: Fray (+13.65 pp vs. Baseline) and Blowhole (0 pp) dominate the aggregate. AROMA's Fray (0.9499) is −0.75 pp below Random (0.9574), suggesting that context-aware placement offers no benefit in the near-ceiling regime.

**MVTec Leather (Table 10, Figure 4.3-3)** presents class-heterogeneous behavior despite monotone background. Random adds +2.22 pp, but AROMA declines −2.69 pp vs. Baseline (0.8052 vs. 0.8321), a −4.91 pp gap from Random. Per-class: Fold (+15.53 pp AROMA vs. Baseline, but −9.95 pp vs. Random) dominates the aggregates, while Glue and Poke deteriorate (−3.07 pp and −14.92 pp AROMA vs. Baseline). The negative aggregate has a concrete mechanism: on this near-uniform surface the similarity-ranked background assignment collapses onto a small background pool (15 distinct normal backgrounds across 400 AROMA composites, versus 202 for Random), so AROMA loses background diversity precisely where placement position carries no compatibility signal. Within-class heterogeneity therefore does not translate into a usable cross-class compatibility signal, and the diversity loss tips the aggregate negative.

Across the three multi-class datasets, the AROMA−random gap responds to baseline headroom and surface heterogeneity: Severstal (heterogeneous, low-baseline, +1.06 pp) shows positive direction; MTD (near-ceiling, limited leverage, −0.25 pp) converges; MVTec Leather (monotone background, misaligned class variation, −4.91 pp) reverses. Combined with single-class results in §4.2 (AITeX +4.96 pp; Kolektor −0.68 pp), the pattern confirms context-aware placement operates conditionally on both sufficient headroom and informative surface heterogeneity.
