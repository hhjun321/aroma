#3. Methodology

##3.1. Experimental Setup
AROMA was evaluated on five publicly available industrial anomaly detection datasets. These datasets were selected to span a broad range of morphological and contextual complexity. This breadth ensured that the automatic policy-selection mechanism was exercised across distinct complexity regimes rather than a single visual regime. The roster was organized along a class-granularity axis into two single-class datasets — AITeX (a directional, periodic woven-fabric texture; the raw 4096×256 px strips have a 16:1 aspect ratio that collapses under a square-letterbox detector, so AITeX is evaluated under a tiled protocol — 256×256 tiles at stride 128, i.e. 50% overlap, as a single defect class) and Kolektor (KolektorSDD electrical-commutator metal surface, tall ~2.5:1 frames, evaluated as a single defect class) — and three multi-class datasets: Severstal (full-frame steel surface, 1600×256 px, four defect classes with large foreground extent), MTD (machined magnetic-tile ceramic surface, five defect classes), and MVTec Leather (an organic surface texture, five defect classes). Together they covered metal, steel, textile, machined-ceramic, and organic surfaces, providing a heterogeneous test of the framework's claim to generalize without per-dataset re-engineering. The two single-class datasets were intended to play a control role. Because both fixed class granularity at one, AITeX (high context complexity, CCI 0.44) and Kolektor (low context complexity, CCI 0.22) were chosen to vary surface heterogeneity while holding class granularity fixed. In practice, however, this control proved only partial: Kolektor's defects turned out near-trivially detectable, so its downstream baseline came out near-ceiling and it lacks the detection headroom needed for any placement effect to register (§4.4). Kolektor therefore does not cleanly isolate context complexity, since it differs from AITeX in baseline headroom as well as in surface heterogeneity. Consequently, we treat baseline headroom and context complexity (CCI) as jointly operative factors, both required for a downstream gain, rather than claiming a confound-free control. This five-dataset roster is the canonical evaluation set; the synthesis pipeline applied to it is described in §3.2 (a training-free copy-paste compositing engine with gradient-domain seamless/alpha blending, guided by a data-driven symmetric compatibility placement gate), and all downstream results are reported in §4.4. The complexity indices span a broad range (MCI 0.33–0.60, CCI 0.20–0.44), confirming that the roster exercises the policy-selection mechanism across distinct complexity regimes. As shown in Figure 2, which plots the five datasets in the MCI–CCI plane, these regimes are visually distinct: AITeX occupies the high-CCI corner while MVTec Leather sits at high MCI / low CCI, and no two datasets cluster together. Each point is annotated with the morphology/context policy the framework selected for that dataset, making the per-dataset, data-driven policy assignment explicit. AITeX has the highest contextual complexity (directional/periodic texture, CCI 0.44), whereas Kolektor and MVTec Leather sit at the low-CCI end (≈0.20–0.22) where the background surface is comparatively uniform. In contrast, MVTec Leather carries the highest morphological complexity, and the number of profiled defect instances differs by nearly two orders of magnitude across datasets (52 for Kolektor to 3620 for Severstal). Across the five datasets, the morphology axis selects the Otsu policy on four datasets and GMM on Kolektor, whereas the context axis selects GMM or percentile partitioning depending on the dataset—an illustration of the per-dataset, data-driven policy selection that the framework performs without manual tuning.

For each dataset, three experimental conditions were compared under an identical evaluation protocol. The baseline condition employed only the real training partition. The random-ROI and AROMA-ROI conditions both augmented that partition with synthetic defect images produced by the **same training-free copy-paste compositing engine and the same seamless-blending and clean-background gate**; they differ only in the ROI-selection/placement policy — uniform random placement for Random versus the compatibility-aware, data-driven policy of §3.2.6 for AROMA — so that any downstream difference between them isolates ROI selection rather than synthesis quality or volume. Both augmented arms use an identical per-dataset synthetic budget (n_synth parity). All three conditions shared the same real test set, and synthetic imagery was confined exclusively to the training partition to preserve strict train–test isolation.
