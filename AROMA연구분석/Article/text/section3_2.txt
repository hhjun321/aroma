#3. Methodology

##3.2. AROMA Pipeline
The AROMA pipeline consists of sequential stages that progress from dataset pro-filing and complexity quantification to automated policy selection, defect synthesis, and quality control. Unlike its predecessor CASDA, which relied on a handcrafted compati-bility matrix and domain-specific rules tailored to a single dataset, AROMA derives its augmentation policy directly from measurable dataset statistics. This transition is enabled by two data-driven components. First, a profiling stage measures defect morphology and background context distributions and constructs a defect–background compatibility model from their co-occurrence. Second, a complexity analysis stage computes the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and Context Complexity Index (CCI), which are used to automatically select dataset-specific modeling and placement policies. Together, these components replace CASDA's handcrafted compatibility matrix and manually tuned morphological rules, and are recomputed for each dataset without dataset-specific constants.
**Figure 1.** AROMA pipeline architecture and data flow. Inputs are the five industrial datasets (severstal, mvtec_leather, mtd, aitex, kolektor); profiling and complexity analysis quantify dataset characteristics (MCI, CCI) that drive Meta Policy Generator selection of the clustering and placement strategies. ROI selection ranks placement candidates by a realism score, and an offline step assigns clean backgrounds. Real defect crops are then expanded into subtype-specific elastic-warp variants — a training-free copy-paste operation with no learned generative model — before the AROMA arm (copy-paste composition, symmetric compatibility gate, clean-background gate, seamless blending) and the random arm (naive placement baseline) are synthesized and used to train the downstream detector.

As shown in Figure 1, the pipeline flows from the input datasets through profiling and complexity quantification, Meta Policy Generator selection, ROI selection, clean-background assignment, and elastic-warp variant generation, and finally into two synthesis arms (AROMA and random) whose outputs, together with the real data, form the baseline, random-augmented, and AROMA-augmented training conditions fed to the downstream detector. Each stage emits auditable artifacts (JSON metadata and image outputs) to enable reproducibility and transparency. The overall architecture demonstrates how measurable dataset complexity drives policy selection, replacing fixed hand-tuned rules with data-driven decisions. The following subsections detail each stage in turn.

###3.2.1 Phase 0: Dataset Complexity Analysis
The pipeline begins by quantifying the intrinsic complexity of each dataset along two orthogonal axes: defect morphology and background context. Two scalar indices were computed for this purpose. The Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) characterizes the structural variability of defect-bearing regions and was defined as the mean of four min-max-normalized components: 
MCI = Mean(Entropy, ValleyCount, ClassDiversity, 1 − Silhouette).

Here, Entropy captures intensity-distribution disorder, ValleyCount reflects the multimodality of the morphology-feature histograms, ClassDiversity is the effective number of defect types, and the Silhouette term measures cluster separability, with its complement (1 − Silhouette) increasing as morphological groupings become less separa-ble. The Context Complexity Index (CCI) characterizes background heterogeneity and was defined as the mean of four normalized components:
CCI = Mean(TextureEntropy, ContextClusterCount, FreqComplexity, OrientVariance),

Where TextureEntropy quantifies local texture disorder, ContextClusterCount is the number of background context clusters (fitted by a Gaussian mixture with BIC model se-lection), FreqComplexity captures spectral richness in the frequency domain, and Ori-entVariance reflects the dispersion of dominant gradient orientations. The morphology valley count entering MCI was computed as the sum of the per-feature valley counts across the profiled morphology features. Contextual statistics were estimated from a sub-sample of 20,000 image patches per dataset. The resulting MCI and CCI values were used to drive subsequent policy selection, providing a quantitative substitute for the domain expertise previously required by CASDA. 
**Figure 2.** Complexity landscape: MCI-CCI plane across five datasets, with selected policies labeled per dataset.

As shown in the complexity landscape, the evaluated datasets span a broad and well-separated range of morphological and contextual difficulty rather than clustering in a single regime; this spread is what enables the policy-selection mechanism to be exercised across distinct complexity conditions.

###3.2.2 Meta Policy Generator
The Meta Policy Generator translates the complexity analysis into a concrete aug-mentation policy through three stages. First, it analyzes the morphology and context fea-ture distributions obtained during profiling, detecting their modality (valley structure) to determine an appropriate set of candidate policies for each dataset. Second, candidate clustering and partitioning strategies are generated for the morphology (Gaussian mixture or Otsu thresholding) and context (Gaussian mixture or percentile partitioning) axes. For morphology, the candidate set is further pruned using the measured valley counts and MCI, eliminating strategies inconsistent with the observed complexity regime, whereas the context axis retains a fixed candidate set. Finally, the remaining candidates are evaluated on a small representative subset, and the best-performing policy is selected automatically. Because every decision is driven solely by measured dataset statistics rather than domain-specific heuristics, the resulting policy is reproducible and generalizes across heterogeneous datasets. 
**Figure 3.** Empirical evaluation scores for candidate morphology and context policies across the five evaluation datasets. For each dataset, the two-member context-axis pool (Gaussian mixture and percentile partitioning) and the morphology-axis pool (shaped by per-feature valley counts and gated by MCI thresholds) were evaluated by silhouette separability. The selected policy for each dataset (marked with □) was determined entirely by measured complexity statistics rather than manual tuning, making the policy decision auditable and per-dataset. The Otsu morphology policy was selected on four datasets (AITeX, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) and Gaussian mixture on Kolektor; the context axis selected either Gaussian mixture (AITeX, Kolektor, MVTec Leather) or percentile partitioning (Severstal, MTD) depending on the measured context complexity regime.

As shown in Figure 3, this selection procedure is fully auditable: the empirical evaluation scores of all candidate policies across all five datasets are plotted (morphology axis on the left, context axis on the right per dataset), with the selected policy marked (□) in each case. The outcome confirms that on the evaluated roster the morphology axis selected the Otsu policy on four of the five datasets and GMM on Kolektor, whereas the context axis selected Gaussian-mixture partitioning on some datasets and percentile partitioning on others (see §3.1), with the choice driven entirely by the measured complexity statistics rather than fixed by hand. This fully automated, auditable policy selection is the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets without per-dataset hand-tuning.

###3.2.3 Stage 1: ROI Extraction
Regions of interest (ROIs) were extracted from normal images to identify plausible defect placement locations. Otsu thresholding was first applied to generate a foreground mask, followed by connected-component analysis to obtain candidate ROIs, with com-ponents smaller than 100 pixels discarded as noise. For each retained ROI, a five-stage background analysis pipeline classified the surrounding texture into one of five catego-ries—smooth, directional, periodic, organic, or complex—using a fixed heuristic threshold cascade applied identically to every dataset (Table A). A continuity score and a stability score were also computed to quantify the spatial coherence of each ROI and its consistency across the normal-image population, respectively.
**Table A.** Background texture categories from ROI extraction (§3.2.3). Each 64-pixel patch is classified by a fixed heuristic decision cascade whose thresholds are global constants — identical across all datasets rather than per-dataset percentiles. The category label is stored in roi_metadata.json but serves only as an optional input to the subtype-matching quality proxy (active when `--min_quality > 0`); core placement scoring instead operates on morphology clusters and a 64-pixel context-cell grid (§3.2.6), so this auxiliary label is largely orthogonal to the data-driven placement decision.

| Category | Definition | Computational Criteria (fixed thresholds, evaluated in order on each 64-pixel patch; identical across datasets) |
|----------|---|---|
| Smooth | Uniform background, minimal texture variation. | `Variance < 100`. Flat, featureless regions (e.g., bare metal). |
| Directional | Linear, one-directional structure. | else `GradientOrientationEntropy < 1.0` (entropy of an 8-bin Sobel gradient-orientation histogram). Coherent directional edges (e.g., brushed metal, textile weave). |
| Periodic | Repeating spatial patterns or grids. | else `AutocorrelationPeak > 0.15` (off-origin FFT autocorrelation peak). Regular repeats (e.g., tiles, checker patterns). |
| Organic | Natural, irregular structure. | else `LBP_Entropy > 2.5` (uniform local binary pattern, P=8/R=1). Irregular texture without dominant orientation (e.g., leather, wood, fabric). |
| Complex | Multi-feature, heterogeneous regions. | else (fallback when none of the above criteria fire). Boundaries, composite surfaces. |

###3.2.4 Stage 1b: Seed Defect Classification
Seed defects were extracted from defective images to provide morphological tem-plates for synthesis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM) was used to generate precise defect masks, with Otsu thresholding serving as a fallback when SAM segmentation was unavailable or unreliable. Each defect was then classified into one of five generic mor-phological subtypes—linear_scratch, elongated, compact_blob, irregular, or general—using threshold criteria based on linearity, aspect ratio, and solidity. These subtype labels subsequently parameterize the subtype-specific elastic warping strategies described in Section 3.2.5, without relying on domain-specific defect categories. 
**Table B.** Defect subtype classification criteria (§3.2.4). Each seed defect is assigned to one of five subtypes based on morphology metrics (linearity, aspect ratio, solidity). Subtype labels parameterize the subtype-specific elastic-warping displacement fields in Stage 2 (§3.2.5).

| Subtype | Definition | Classification Criteria |
|---------|-----------|-------------------------|
| linear_scratch | Long, thin, linear defect (e.g., scratch, crack). | `Linearity > 0.9` AND `AspectRatio > 5`. Strong directional coherence with extreme elongation. |
| elongated | Elongated but less linear defect (e.g., elongated blob, seam). | `Linearity > 0.7` AND `AspectRatio ∈ [2, 5]` AND `Solidity < 0.85`. Moderate to high linearity with moderate elongation. |
| compact_blob | Compact, nearly isotropic defect (e.g., hole, dent, blob). | `Linearity < 0.6` AND `Solidity > 0.85` AND `AspectRatio < 2`. Low linearity, high solidity, near-circular footprint. |
| irregular | Irregular, medium-linearity defect (e.g., stain, patch, boundary artifact). | `Linearity ∈ [0.4, 0.8]` AND `Solidity ∈ [0.65, 0.95]` AND `AspectRatio < 4`. Mixed morphological properties, intermediate geometry. |
| general | Fallback category for ambiguous or mixed-morphology defects. | Does not meet criteria for any of the above four categories. Default label; used when sub-type assignment is uncertain or defect lacks clear morphological signature. |

**Figure 4.** Morphology feature distributions across datasets (severstal, mvtec_leather, mtd, aitex). Box plots of linearity, solidity, and aspect ratio values from defect instances show the range of morphological properties across the four benchmark datasets. Red dashed threshold lines (from Table B) indicate the decision boundaries for subtype classification. The spread confirms that each subtype occupies distinct feature space regions, validating the threshold-based classification approach.

As shown in Table 4, the ROI selection proceeds through three sequential phases: quota assignment, multi-class gating, and compatibility-based ranking.
Placement candidates are ranked using a realism score,

ROI score= 0.5⋅ctxprior+0.3⋅morphprior+0.2⋅qualityscore

where ctx_prior represents the profiled defect–background compatibility, morph_prior the morphology-cluster prior, and quality_score the subtype-to-background matching score. To prevent morphological collapse, each source defect crop is selected at most once. Before synthesis, a clean background is assigned to each ROI by ranking candidate backgrounds using histogram-matching cues derived from the profiling stage, including source overlap, class-conditional overlap, and size compatibility.

During composition, placement is governed by a symmetric compatibility gate,

Where P_def and P_clean denote the profiled occurrence probabilities of the defect and clean-background patches, respectively, and ε is a Laplace smoothing term. The square-root formulation ensures symmetric weighting of the defect and background probabilities. Each candidate defect crop is evaluated against the 64-pixel tiled neigh-borhood of the target ROI, and the resulting compatibility score determines the placement location. This data-driven compatibility gate automatically replaces CASDA's handcrafted compatibility matrix with a dataset-specific counterpart derived directly from the profiling statistics. In contrast, the Random baseline performs no compatibility-based ranking and differs only in the ROI placement policy.

###3.2.6 Blending Synthesis
Each selected defect crop is placed at the designated bounding box on the target normal image. The same blend operator is applied identically to the AROMA and random arms, ensuring blending is not a confounding factor. Boundary artifacts are measured in the quality gate (Section 3.2.8). For each synthesized image, the corresponding defect mask and bounding box are co-saved, providing pixel-level localization for downstream de-tection.

3.2.7 Quality Gate
A quality gate filters low-fidelity synthetic samples prior to dataset construction. Two primary metrics are computed for each composite: artifact score (high-frequency content at defect edges from compositing) and blur score (local sharpness via Laplacian variance ≥ 100). A composite quality score Q is derived as:
Q= 0.30 · blur + 0.30 · contrast + 0.20 · brightness + 0.20 · noise

Where each component is normalized to [0, 1]. Both metrics are evaluated over a di-lated ROI mask centered on the defect placement, confining scoring to the defect neigh-borhood rather than the entire image. Samples with Q < 0.7 are discarded; those with Q ≥ 0.7 are retained.
