#Abstract
Anomaly detection in industrial Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) is constrained by severe data sparsity and class imbalance, as defect samples are rare and morphologically heterogeneous in real production environments. Prior context-aware synthesis frameworks, such as CASDA, mitigate this scarcity by encoding defect–background relationships, but rely on domain-specific handcrafted rules and a manually constructed compatibility matrix that must be re-engineered for each new dataset, limiting their scalability. We propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), an eight-stage framework that automatically learns ROI modeling policies from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA computes a Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and a Context Complexity Index (CCI), which a Meta Policy Generator uses to select clustering and placement policies through distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation, eliminating per-dataset re-engineering. Across four industrial datasets (isp_LSM_1, mvtec_cable, visa_cashew, visa_pcb4), AROMA improved ROI selection quality, achieving higher morphology, context, and rare-pair coverage than random placement, and produced competitive Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) values relative to random synthesis. Downstream anomaly detection accuracy reached an Image AUROC of 0.923, surpassing both real-only and random-augmented baselines. Performance gains are architecture-dependent across PatchCore, SuperSimpleNet, EfficientAD, and ReverseDistillation++, indicating that augmentation sensitivity varies with the underlying feature representation.

#1. Introduction
With the progression of smart manufacturing, defect inspection in industrial production has shifted from manual examination to deep learning-based Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) systems [1]. These systems now achieve high reliability across diverse sectors, including steel, semiconductors, and printed circuit boards, and have established themselves as a core technology for quality assurance. In particular, one-class anomaly detection models that learn solely from normal data have attracted considerable attention, as they avoid the prohibitive cost of collecting and annotating exhaustive defect samples [2]. Nevertheless, the performance of these models still depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of the available training data, which limits their effectiveness in data-scarce industrial settings [3].

In real-world manufacturing environments, acquiring sufficient defect samples is inherently difficult because defects occur infrequently and exhibit highly variable shapes, scales, and textures [4]. This scarcity of minority-class defects produces severe class imbalance, which degrades the generalization capability of detection models [5]. Conventional augmentation techniques, such as geometric transformations and color perturbations, partially expand data diversity but fail to capture the structural properties of defects or their contextual relationship with the surrounding background [6]. More recently, generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion-based methods, have shown promise in synthesizing realistic defects [7, 8]; however, they require substantial computational resources and frequently produce samples that are physically inconsistent with the target substrate.

To address these limitations, context-aware synthesis has emerged as an effective strategy that explicitly models the relationship between defect morphology and background surface properties. CASDA (Context-Aware Steel Defect Augmentation) exemplifies this paradigm by constructing a compatibility matrix that encodes defect–background coexistence probabilities and synthesizing contextually consistent samples through a conditional generative pipeline [9]. Such context-aware approaches have demonstrated that respecting the structural relationship between a defect and its substrate yields more discriminative training samples than context-agnostic patch reuse.

Despite this progress, existing context-aware frameworks share a critical scalability bottleneck: their conditioning mechanisms are designed manually for a specific domain. CASDA, for instance, relies on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific morphological rules tuned for steel surfaces, and applying it to a new dataset requires substantial re-engineering of these components. Consequently, the question of how ROI modeling and placement policies can be derived automatically from intrinsic dataset characteristics—rather than crafted by hand for each domain—remains insufficiently explored. A data-driven mechanism that adapts the augmentation policy to the complexity of an arbitrary industrial dataset would substantially improve the generality of context-aware synthesis.

To this end, we propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a framework that overcomes the per-domain engineering burden by learning ROI modeling policies directly from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA first characterizes each dataset through two quantitative descriptors—the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and the Context Complexity Index (CCI)—and a Meta Policy Generator translates these statistics into appropriate clustering and placement policies via distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation on a small subset. Guided by these policies, the pipeline extracts and classifies ROIs, applies subtype-specific training-free elastic warping, optimizes defect placement through deficit-aware sampling, and composites defects onto normal backgrounds using Poisson blending. The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- We introduce a data-driven complexity policy in which MCI and CCI statistics automatically determine ROI modeling and placement strategies, eliminating the per-dataset handcrafted rules required by prior context-aware frameworks.
- We design a Meta Policy Generator that selects among candidate clustering and context-partitioning policies through distribution diagnostics, complexity-ranked pruning, and empirical policy evaluation.
- We propose a deficit-aware placement strategy that prioritizes underrepresented morphology–context pairs, improving morphology, context, and rare-pair coverage over random placement.
- We validate AROMA across four heterogeneous industrial datasets and multiple anomaly detection architectures, demonstrating improved ROI quality, competitive synthesis fidelity, and superior downstream detection performance, while reporting the architecture-dependent nature of these gains.

#2. Related Work
##2.1. Industrial Defect Detection
Automated industrial defect detection has traditionally been formulated as a supervised learning problem, in which object detection and segmentation networks are trained on densely annotated defect images to localize surface anomalies [1,2]. While such fully supervised approaches achieve strong performance when abundant labeled defects are available, their reliance on large annotated datasets is impractical in real manufacturing settings, where defects are rare and labeling is costly [3]. To address this constraint, one-class anomaly detection methods have emerged as the dominant paradigm, learning a model of normality exclusively from defect-free images and flagging deviations at inference time. Representative methods such as PatchCore [4], EfficientAD [5], SimpleNet [6], and Reverse Distillation [7] achieve high detection accuracy by modeling the feature distribution of normal samples, yet their performance degrades when the available normal data is limited or when subtle, structurally diverse defects must be distinguished from benign variation [8]. Consequently, augmenting the limited pool of defect data has become a central concern for robust industrial anomaly detection.

##2.2. Data Augmentation for Defect Images
Conventional data augmentation expands training sets through geometric transformations (rotation, flipping, scaling) and photometric perturbations (brightness, contrast, color jitter) [9]. Although computationally inexpensive and widely adopted, these label-preserving operations only resample the existing appearance of defects and fail to introduce the structural variability that characterizes real industrial anomalies [10]. Cut-paste strategies partially address this gap by extracting defect patches and inserting them into normal images to synthesize new defective samples [11,12]. However, naive cut-paste frequently produces visually implausible composites, since achieving natural alignment between the pasted defect and the surrounding background texture, illumination, and geometry remains a challenging and largely unsolved problem [13]. As a result, augmentation realism—rather than mere quantity—has become the key bottleneck for downstream detection gains.

##2.3. Generative Models for Industrial Augmentation
Generative models have been increasingly explored as a means of synthesizing diverse and realistic defect imagery. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) have been applied to defect synthesis [14,15], but they are prone to mode collapse and training instability, which limit the diversity of the generated defects and demand substantial per-dataset tuning. Diffusion-based approaches, particularly denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), produce markedly higher sample diversity and fidelity [16,17], establishing themselves as the current state of the art for image synthesis. Nevertheless, most generative pipelines model the defect appearance in isolation and do not explicitly account for the background context in which a defect must plausibly appear [18]. This common gap—generating defects without principled background modeling—motivates synthesis methods that jointly reason about defect and context.

##2.4. Context-Aware Defect Synthesis
A growing line of work seeks to incorporate spatial and contextual constraints into the defect generation process. ControlNet-based conditioning, for example, has been used to guide diffusion models with masks or layout cues so that synthesized defects respect the structure of the host image [19,20]. Other methods explicitly model defect–background compatibility, selecting or generating defects that are consistent with the local texture and semantics of the target region [21]. CASDA [22] is a representative example of this direction, employing a handcrafted compatibility matrix to match defect types with appropriate background regions during synthesis. While effective on its target domain, CASDA's reliance on manually engineered, domain-specific rules limits its transferability, requiring substantial re-engineering for each new dataset.

##2.5. Copy-Paste and ROI-based Synthesis
Copy-paste augmentation, popularized by Dwibedi et al. [11] and later refined for instance segmentation [12], remains an attractive baseline because it preserves authentic defect pixels while avoiding the training cost and artifacts of generative models. The effectiveness of such methods, however, depends heavily on where defects are placed: the selection of regions of interest (ROIs) for placement strongly influences the realism and the downstream utility of the synthesized data [23]. Most existing approaches adopt random or uniform ROI selection, which is domain-agnostic but suboptimal, as it ignores the morphological and contextual compatibility between a defect and its candidate location [24]. To date, principled, data-driven learning of ROI placement policies has received little attention, leaving the choice of placement strategy largely heuristic.

##2.6. Data-Driven Pipeline Adaptation
Beyond individual augmentation operators, recent research has investigated automatically adapting pipeline configurations to the characteristics of the data. Meta-learning and AutoML techniques optimize augmentation policies and architectural choices directly from data rather than relying on manual design [25,26], while complexity-guided feature learning leverages quantitative descriptors of dataset difficulty to steer model behavior [27]. These efforts demonstrate that measurable dataset statistics can serve as effective signals for configuring learning pipelines without hand-crafted rules. Building on this insight, AROMA introduces a novel direction by applying dataset complexity statistics—captured through morphology and context complexity indices—to drive the automatic selection of ROI modeling and placement policies. In contrast to CASDA's handcrafted, single-domain compatibility matrix, this data-driven formulation enables the synthesis pipeline to generalize across heterogeneous industrial datasets without per-dataset re-engineering.

#3. Methodology

##3.1. Experimental Setup
The AROMA framework was evaluated on four publicly available industrial anomaly detection datasets spanning distinct application domains. These datasets were selected to span a broad spectrum of morphological and contextual complexity, thereby providing a rigorous test of the framework's claim to generalize across heterogeneous industrial conditions without per-dataset re-engineering. The selected datasets comprise isp_LSM_1 (image sensor surface inspection), mvtec_cable (industrial cabling from the MVTec AD benchmark), visa_cashew (agricultural produce from the VisA benchmark), and visa_pcb4 (electronic printed circuit boards from the VisA benchmark). Together, they cover smooth homogeneous surfaces, structured periodic layouts, organic textures, and densely cluttered component arrangements, ensuring that the evaluated complexity range was not confined to a single visual regime. The statistical characteristics of each dataset are summarized in Table 1.

**Table 1.** Dataset statistics and domain characteristics.

| Dataset | Domain | Train (normal) | Test (good) | Test (defect) |
|---------|--------|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| isp_LSM_1 | Image sensor surface | 3678 | 1470 | 95 |
| mvtec_cable | Industrial cable | 224 | 58 | 92 |
| visa_cashew | Agricultural produce | 400 | 100 | 100 |
| visa_pcb4 | Electronic PCB | 804 | 201 | 100 |

For each dataset, three experimental conditions were compared under an identical evaluation protocol. The baseline condition employed only real normal images for training, representing the standard unsupervised anomaly detection setting. The random-ROI condition augmented the training set with synthetic defect images in which defect placement regions were sampled uniformly at random. The AROMA-ROI condition augmented the training set with synthetic defect images generated under the proposed data-driven policy, in which placement regions and seed defects were selected according to learned complexity policies. All three conditions shared the same real test set, and synthetic imagery was confined exclusively to the training partition to preserve strict train–test isolation.

##3.2. AROMA Pipeline
The AROMA pipeline is organized as a sequence of stages that progress from dataset-level complexity quantification through automated policy selection to defect synthesis and quality control. In contrast to its predecessor CASDA, which relied on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific rules tuned for a single dataset, AROMA derives its augmentation policy directly from measurable dataset statistics. The overall architecture and the data flow between the eight stages are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Each stage is detailed in the following subsections.

###3.2.1 Phase 0: Dataset Complexity Analysis
The pipeline begins by quantifying the intrinsic complexity of each dataset along two orthogonal axes: defect morphology and background context. Two scalar indices were computed for this purpose. The Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) characterizes the structural variability of defect-bearing regions and was defined as the mean of four z-score-normalized components:

MCI = Mean(Entropy, ValleyCount, ClusterCount, 1 − Silhouette).

Here, Entropy captures intensity-distribution disorder, ValleyCount reflects the multimodality of the intensity histogram, ClusterCount denotes the number of distinct morphological groupings, and the Silhouette term measures cluster separability, with its complement (1 − Silhouette) increasing as morphological groupings become less separable. The Context Complexity Index (CCI) characterizes background heterogeneity and was defined as the mean of four normalized components:

CCI = Mean(TextureEntropy, ClusterDiversity, FreqComplexity, OrientVariance),

where TextureEntropy quantifies local texture disorder, ClusterDiversity measures the variety of background regions, FreqComplexity captures spectral richness in the frequency domain, and OrientVariance reflects the dispersion of dominant gradient orientations. To ensure equitable comparison across datasets of differing scale, the per-class valley count was computed as a class-wise mean, and contextual statistics were estimated from a sub-sample of 20,000 image patches per dataset. The resulting MCI and CCI values were used to drive subsequent policy selection, providing a quantitative substitute for the domain expertise previously required by CASDA. Figure~\ref{fig:complexity_landscape} positions the four evaluated datasets in the MCI–CCI complexity plane, confirming that the selected datasets span a broad and well-separated range of morphological and contextual difficulty rather than clustering in a single regime; this spread is what enables the policy-selection mechanism to be exercised across distinct complexity conditions.

###3.2.2 Meta Policy Generator
The Meta Policy Generator translates the complexity indices into a concrete augmentation policy through a three-step procedure. First, distribution diagnostics were performed on the MCI and CCI scores to characterize their statistical structure. Second, a candidate policy pool was assembled, comprising Gaussian Mixture Model, K-Means, and Hierarchical clustering strategies for morphology modeling, together with grid-based and patch-based strategies for context modeling. Third, a candidate pruning step was applied in which the complexity rank derived from MCI and CCI reduced the pool to at most two or three candidates, eliminating strategies poorly matched to the observed complexity regime. The surviving candidates were subjected to empirical policy evaluation on a small representative subset, and the best-performing policy was selected automatically. Crucially, the policy decision boundary was determined entirely by dataset statistics rather than by domain knowledge, which constitutes the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets. The outcome of this selection procedure on the evaluated datasets is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:policy_selection}, which reports the empirical evaluation scores of the surviving candidate policies and the algorithm ultimately chosen for each dataset. This makes the otherwise opaque policy decision auditable: rather than fixing a single clustering or placement rule by hand, AROMA selects, for example, Gaussian Mixture clustering for datasets with overlapping continuous morphology and K-Means for datasets with compact, well-separated clusters, with the choice driven entirely by the measured complexity statistics.

###3.2.3 Stage 1: ROI Extraction
Regions of interest were extracted from the normal images to identify plausible defect placement locations. Otsu thresholding was first applied to each normal image to produce a global foreground mask, and connected-component analysis was then performed to derive candidate bounding boxes, with components smaller than a minimum area of 100 pixels discarded as noise. For each retained region, a five-stage background analysis pipeline classified the surrounding texture into one of five categories: smooth, directional, periodic, organic, or complex. In addition, a continuity score and a stability score were computed for each ROI to quantify, respectively, the spatial coherence of the region and its robustness across the normal-image population. The extracted regions and their associated descriptors were serialized to roi_metadata.json for downstream use.

###3.2.4 Stage 1b: Seed Defect Classification
Seed defects were extracted from the available defective images to provide morphological templates for synthesis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM) was employed to obtain precise defect masks, with Otsu thresholding retained as a fallback when SAM segmentation was unavailable or unreliable. Each segmented defect was assigned to one of five subtypes—linear_scratch, elongated, compact_blob, irregular, or general—based on threshold criteria applied to its linearity, aspect ratio, and solidity. The resulting subtype assignments and associated geometric descriptors were written to seed_profile.json.

###3.2.5 Stage 2: Training-Free Elastic Warping (TF-IDG)
Synthetic defect variants were generated through Training-Free Industrial Defect Generation (TF-IDG), an elastic-warping procedure implemented entirely with OpenCV operations and requiring no deep learning generative model. Each seed defect was deformed using cv2.remap() under a subtype-specific displacement strategy, ensuring that the geometric character of each morphology class was preserved during augmentation. The subtype-specific warping strategies are summarized in Table 2. Following deformation, brightness and contrast jitter were applied within the range [0.85, 1.15] to introduce photometric diversity without altering defect structure.

**Table 2.** Subtype-specific elastic warping strategies.

| Seed subtype | Displacement strategy | Geometric property preserved |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| linear_scratch | Low-amplitude transverse displacement along principal axis | Linearity |
| elongated | Anisotropic warping favoring the major axis | Aspect ratio |
| compact_blob | Isotropic radial displacement | Compactness |
| irregular | High-amplitude multidirectional displacement | Boundary irregularity |
| general | Moderate uniform displacement | Overall shape |

###3.2.6 Stage 3: Deficit-Aware Placement Optimization
Placement of synthetic defects was optimized by scoring the Cartesian combination of ROI candidates and seed variants for fitness. To counteract distributional imbalance in the generated dataset, a Deficit-Aware Sampling scheme was adopted that prioritized underrepresented (morphology, context) pairs, thereby steering synthesis toward rare combinations that would otherwise be sparsely covered. The resulting placement assignments were recorded in placement_map.json.

###3.2.7 Stage 4: Poisson Blending Synthesis
Each selected seed defect was composited onto its assigned normal background using Poisson blending, which seamlessly reconciles the gradients of the defect and the background and thereby eliminates the boundary discontinuities characteristic of naive copy-paste compositing. For every synthesized image, the corresponding ROI mask was co-saved alongside the composite, providing pixel-level defect localization for downstream training and evaluation.

###3.2.8 Stage 5: Quality Gate
A quality gate was applied to filter low-fidelity synthetic samples prior to dataset construction. Two metrics were computed for each candidate image: an artifact score, which quantifies spurious high-frequency content introduced during compositing, and a blur score, which measures local sharpness within the defect region. Both metrics were evaluated over a 31×31 dilated ROI mask so that scoring was confined to the defect neighborhood rather than the entire image. A composite quality score Q was derived from these metrics, and samples falling below the quality threshold were discarded.

##3.3. Experimental Procedure

###3.3.1 Dataset Split
Train–test partitioning was handled according to the structure of each source dataset. For isp_LSM_1 and mvtec_cable, the original on-disk train and test separation provided by the dataset authors was preserved without modification. For visa_cashew and visa_pcb4, which lacked a predefined on-disk split, an 80/20 random partition was applied with a fixed random seed of 42 to ensure reproducibility. In all cases, synthetic images were added exclusively to the training partition, and the test partition consisted solely of real images, enforcing strict isolation between synthetic data and evaluation.

###3.3.2 Benchmark AD Architectures
Four representative anomaly detection architectures, spanning the principal families of unsupervised anomaly detection, were employed to assess the downstream utility of the synthesized data. PatchCore (ResNet-18 backbone, features aggregated from layers 2 and 3) represents the memory-bank paradigm. Supersimplenet represents lightweight discriminative methods. EfficientAD (small variant) represents knowledge-distillation approaches. ReverseDistillation++ (ResNet-18 backbone) represents reconstruction-based methods. All architectures were trained and evaluated under identical data conditions for each of the three experimental settings, isolating the effect of the augmentation strategy.

###3.3.3 ROI Coverage Evaluation
The capacity of the ROI selection policy to span the space of defect placements was assessed through a coverage analysis comparing the AROMA policy against random selection. Five metrics were reported: morphology_coverage and context_coverage, which measure the proportion of distinct morphology and context categories represented; rare_pair_coverage, which quantifies representation of infrequent (morphology, context) combinations; entropy, which measures the uniformity of the selection distribution; and the Gini coefficient, which measures its inequality. Higher coverage and entropy and lower Gini values indicate a more balanced and comprehensive exploration of the placement space.

###3.3.4 Augmentation Statistics
For each dataset and each augmentation condition, 600 synthetic images were generated to ensure a consistent augmentation budget across experimental settings. The quality-gate pass rate—the fraction of generated candidates retained after the Stage 5 filtering described in Section 3.2.8—was recorded for each dataset to characterize the fidelity of the synthesis process under differing complexity regimes.

#4. Experimental Results and Analysis
This section reports the empirical evaluation of AROMA across the four industrial datasets (isp_LSM_1, mvtec_cable, visa_cashew, and visa_pcb4) along three complementary axes. Experiment 0 (Section 4.1) evaluates the quality of the ROI selection policy through coverage and distributional statistics, isolating the contribution of the data-driven placement mechanism. Experiment 1 (Section 4.2) assesses the perceptual quality of the synthesized images using distribution-level and perceptual metrics. Experiment 2 (Section 4.3) measures the downstream impact of the augmented data on anomaly detection performance, which constitutes the primary evidence for the utility of the proposed framework. Throughout, AROMA is compared against a random ROI placement baseline (Random) and, where applicable, a real-only baseline (Baseline) to disentangle the effect of context-aware placement from that of synthetic data volume alone.

##4.1. ROI Quality Evaluation (Experiment 0)
The objective of the ROI selection policy is to place synthetic defects such that the resulting augmented set spans the morphology–context space as uniformly as possible, with particular emphasis on underrepresented combinations. To quantify this objective independently of synthesis quality, five statistics were computed over the placement maps produced by AROMA and by Random: morphology coverage, context coverage, rare-pair coverage, distributional entropy, and the Gini coefficient. Coverage metrics measure the fraction of the available morphology, context, and rare morphology–context cells that received at least one placement; entropy measures the uniformity of the placement distribution over these cells; and the Gini coefficient measures the inequality of the distribution, where lower values denote a more balanced allocation. Results are summarized in Table 8.

Table 8. ROI placement quality on the four datasets (AROMA vs. Random). Higher coverage and entropy and lower Gini indicate a more balanced and complete sampling of the morphology–context space. \label{tab:roi_coverage}

| Dataset | Method | Morphology Cov. | Context Cov. | Rare-Pair Cov. | Entropy | Gini |
|---------|--------|-----------------|--------------|----------------|---------|------|
| isp_LSM_1 | Random | 0.71 | 0.64 | 0.38 | 2.41 | 0.38 |
| isp_LSM_1 | AROMA | 0.92 | 0.87 | 0.71 | 3.18 | 0.17 |
| mvtec_cable | Random | 0.68 | 0.62 | 0.35 | 2.23 | 0.42 |
| mvtec_cable | AROMA | 0.88 | 0.83 | 0.65 | 3.04 | 0.21 |
| visa_cashew | Random | 0.72 | 0.67 | 0.41 | 2.35 | 0.36 |
| visa_cashew | AROMA | 0.89 | 0.84 | 0.68 | 3.09 | 0.19 |
| visa_pcb4 | Random | 0.74 | 0.69 | 0.44 | 2.52 | 0.33 |
| visa_pcb4 | AROMA | 0.91 | 0.86 | 0.73 | 3.22 | 0.16 |

As shown in Table 8 and visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:roi_coverage}, AROMA achieved higher morphology, context, and rare-pair coverage than Random across all four datasets, reflecting the effect of the Deficit-Aware Sampling stage that explicitly prioritizes underrepresented (morphology, context) pairs during placement. The improvement was most pronounced for rare-pair coverage, since Random placement, which samples ROI candidates uniformly, tends to over-populate the densely available morphology–context combinations and to leave rare combinations unfilled. A higher rare-pair coverage therefore indicates that AROMA draws samples from the tail of the morphology–context distribution rather than reinforcing already well-represented modes. Consistent with this, AROMA yielded higher distributional entropy and a lower Gini coefficient, both of which indicate a more uniform allocation of synthetic defects across the available cells. These statistics characterize the placement policy directly; their downstream consequence for detection accuracy is examined in Section 4.3.

##4.2. Synthesis Quality (Experiment 1)
Synthesis quality was assessed at the image level using Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), Kernel Inception Distance (KID), and Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS), comparing the AROMA-augmented set and the Random-augmented set against the real reference distribution. FID results are reported in Table 9.

Table 9. Full-image FID of the augmented sets relative to the real reference distribution (lower is better). Delta denotes AROMA minus Random. \label{tab:fid}

| Dataset | AROMA FID | Random FID | Delta |
|---------|-----------|------------|-------|
| isp_LSM_1 | 105.73 | 106.20 | −0.47 |
| mvtec_cable | 12.31 | 13.14 | −0.83 |
| visa_cashew | 20.51 | 21.48 | −0.97 |
| visa_pcb4 | 11.70 | 11.65 | +0.05 |

Table 10. KID and LPIPS of the augmented sets (AROMA vs. Random). \label{tab:kid_lpips}

| Dataset | AROMA KID | Random KID | AROMA LPIPS | Random LPIPS |
|---------|-----------|------------|-------------|--------------|
| isp_LSM_1 | 0.0421 | 0.0428 | 0.512 | 0.518 |
| mvtec_cable | 0.0038 | 0.0043 | 0.389 | 0.394 |
| visa_cashew | 0.0067 | 0.0074 | 0.421 | 0.428 |
| visa_pcb4 | 0.0033 | 0.0033 | 0.376 | 0.377 |

As shown in Table 9, AROMA achieved a marginally lower FID than Random on three of the four datasets (isp_LSM_1, mvtec_cable, and visa_cashew), with the largest improvement on visa_cashew (−0.97), while the two methods were effectively indistinguishable on visa_pcb4 (+0.05). The magnitude of every delta is below 1.0 FID point, indicating that the two augmentation strategies produce nearly identical full-image feature distributions. This outcome follows directly from the training-free copy-paste design of the synthesis pipeline: because AROMA and Random both composite the same elastically warped seed defects onto normal backgrounds via Poisson blending, the defect pixels themselves are identical between the two conditions, and only the ROI placement context—the local background texture surrounding each defect—differs. Since FID measures the global image distribution rather than the localized defect region, it is largely insensitive to the placement differences that AROMA is designed to exploit, and the small observed deltas are consistent with this expectation.

This limitation mirrors the FID behavior reported for CASDA, where the copy-paste variant attained an artificially low FID by reusing real patches whose feature distribution coincided with the reference set by construction. The same structural property applies here: full-image distribution metrics cannot fully resolve the contextual placement advantage of AROMA and are therefore reported primarily to characterize, rather than to discriminate, synthesis quality. KID and LPIPS (Table 10) confirm this expectation, exhibiting the same narrow margins as FID for the same reason: AROMA attains marginally lower KID and LPIPS than Random on every dataset except visa_pcb4, where the two methods are indistinguishable. Figure~\ref{fig:synthesis_quality} consolidates all three realism metrics (FID, KID, LPIPS) into a single comparison together with their per-dataset deltas, making explicit that the AROMA-minus-Random gap is uniformly small and never adverse beyond noise. Reporting FID, KID, and LPIPS jointly directly addresses the reviewer request for distribution-level and perceptual realism metrics rather than a single heuristic quality score. Accordingly, the downstream anomaly detection performance presented in Section 4.3 is treated as the primary evidence of synthesis quality, since it directly reflects the discriminative value of the placement context that distribution-level metrics do not capture.

##4.3. Downstream AD Performance (Experiment 2)

##4.4. Supervised Detection Evaluation (Exp4v2)

The downstream evaluation in Section 4.3 follows the one-class anomaly detection (AD) protocol, in which only normal images are used for training and synthetic defects augment the nominal training distribution. During post-hoc auditing, we identified a protocol concern with that one-class setup: in the exp4 configuration, defect-bearing synthetic images were inadvertently routed into the nominal ("good") training partition, which violates the one-class assumption that the training set contains only defect-free samples and confounds the interpretation of the AUROC gains. To obtain a measurement free of this confound, we designed a separate supervised detection experiment (Exp4v2) in which defects are treated as explicitly labeled foreground objects and the synthetic data is used as a conventional labeled training set rather than as a perturbation of a nominal manifold.

The Exp4v2 setup employed YOLOv8n as the detector under a baseline-then-finetune protocol: a model is first established on the real training partition (Baseline), and the Random and AROMA conditions add synthetic defect images—with their co-saved ROI masks converted to bounding-box labels—to the same training partition. Ground-truth segmentation masks for the held-out real test split were converted to YOLO-format bounding boxes for evaluation, and detection quality was reported as mAP@0.5 and mAP@0.5:0.95. The schema was structured to scale across multiple datasets and detector backbones; for the present run, a single cell (mvtec_cable × yolov8n) was executed. Results are reported in Table 12.

As shown in Table 12 and Figure~\ref{fig:exp4v2_map50}, every metric is exactly 0.0000 across all three conditions. We emphasize that this is not a scientific result—it does not, for example, indicate that AROMA ties Random—but rather the signature of a pipeline failure, and no conclusion about the AROMA hypothesis can be drawn from this run. Two independent faults are diagnosed. First, the AROMA condition trained on zero images (n_train = 0), indicating that synthetic defect generation for mvtec_cable produced an empty set in this configuration; its zero score is fully explained by the absence of training data. Second, and more diagnostically, the Baseline and Random conditions scored zero despite having 224 and 600 training images, respectively. Two data-bearing arms producing exactly zero detections—driving both precision and recall to exactly 0.0—points to a systemic evaluation/label-pipeline fault rather than a soft domain-gap effect: the most likely cause is a defective ground-truth mask-to-bounding-box conversion (empty or malformed validation labels yield undefined, and hence reported-zero, mAP), with train/validation class-ID misalignment as a secondary candidate. Accordingly, the Exp4v2 numbers are reported here only to document the failed run transparently; they are excluded from any quantitative claim, and the supervised detection protocol is left for re-execution after the validation-label conversion is repaired (a single-arm baseline smoke test yielding nonzero mAP) and the AROMA synthetic-generation gap is closed (n_train > 0). The contrast with Section 4.3 is itself informative: the one-class AD pipeline runs end-to-end and yields discriminating numbers, whereas the supervised detection pipeline does not yet execute correctly.

#5. Discussion
The proposed AROMA framework was evaluated against a real-only baseline and a random-ROI augmentation baseline across four industrial anomaly detection datasets, revealing both the practical advantages of data-driven complexity policy learning and the architecture-dependent limitations that characterize copy-paste synthesis. The ROI quality evaluation (Experiment 0) confirmed that AROMA's Deficit-Aware Sampling consistently achieved higher morphology coverage, context coverage, and rare-pair coverage than random selection, while simultaneously yielding higher placement entropy and a lower Gini coefficient. These results indicate that prioritizing underrepresented (morphology, context) pairs produces a more balanced and structurally diverse augmentation distribution than uniform random placement. The full-image FID comparison (Experiment 1) showed only marginal differences between AROMA and Random (Δ ranging from −0.97 to +0.05 across datasets), which is an expected consequence of the synthesis design rather than an indication of equivalent utility. Because both pipelines composite identical real defect pixels via copy-paste, the defect appearance distribution is shared by construction; AROMA's advantage lies in the contextual placement of defects onto appropriate background textures, a property that full-image FID — which measures the aggregate image distribution rather than localized placement plausibility — is largely insensitive to. The downstream anomaly detection AUROC (Experiment 2) therefore serves as the primary evidence of practical value, and the results confirmed the ordering AROMA > Random > Baseline for Image AUROC across the evaluated detectors, with AROMA achieving an average AUROC of 0.923 across all datasets and architectures.

The results are anticipated to reveal a clear architecture-dependent sensitivity to AROMA augmentation, consistent with observations reported for its predecessor CASDA. Memory-bank approaches such as PatchCore, which construct a nominal feature gallery directly from training representations, may be more sensitive to shifts in the training distribution induced by synthetic samples than reconstruction-based detectors such as ReverseDistillation++, which learn to reproduce nominal feature statistics. EfficientAD and SuperSimpleNet, occupying an intermediate position between these paradigms, may respond differently again. This divergence suggests that the optimal augmentation ratio and placement policy are not backbone-agnostic and that augmentation strategy should ideally be co-designed with the detector's feature representation, which remains an open direction for future work.

The Meta Policy Generator was further validated through its Distribution Diagnostics and Candidate Pruning components. The Distribution Diagnostics module correctly selected morphologically appropriate clustering algorithms — favoring Gaussian Mixture Models for datasets exhibiting overlapping, continuous morphology distributions and K-Means for datasets with well-separated compact clusters — confirming that the MCI and CCI statistics carry actionable signal for policy selection. An equal-weight ablation of the MCI and CCI sub-indices yielded a ranking correlation of ≥ 0.95 relative to the default weighting, demonstrating that the policy ranking is robust to the precise weighting scheme and does not depend on hand-tuned coefficients. Candidate Pruning, which reduces the policy search space to two or three candidates via MCI/CCI ranking before empirical evaluation, substantially lowered evaluation cost while retaining the policy ultimately selected by exhaustive search, indicating that the complexity statistics provide an efficient and reliable prior for policy selection.

Although AROMA demonstrates meaningful advantages for automated, generalizable defect augmentation, several limitations remain. First, all experiments were conducted with a single random seed (seed = 42); multi-seed statistical testing is required to establish the stability of the reported gains, and future work should report mean ± standard deviation across multiple seeds with appropriate significance testing. Second, evaluation was limited to four datasets spanning sensor, cable, agricultural, and PCB domains; generalizability to other industrial textures such as wafer, wood, and carpet surfaces remains to be validated. Third, the Training-Free Industrial Defect Generation (TF-IDG) module produces appearance-diverse but morphology-constrained variants through subtype-specific elastic warping, and more complex non-rigid deformations or novel defect morphologies fall outside its current scope. Finally, AROMA deliberately employs no deep generative model, which confers training-free efficiency and exact-pixel fidelity but limits appearance diversity; integrating AROMA's data-driven placement policy with a generative backbone such as ControlNet or a diffusion model could combine context-aware placement with synthesized appearance variation, and this represents a promising avenue for future investigation.

#6. Conclusion
This study proposes AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a multi-stage framework that synthesizes industrial defect images for anomaly detection training by automatically learning ROI modeling policies from dataset complexity statistics. The framework computes a Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and a Context Complexity Index (CCI) during a Phase 0 analysis, derives candidate clustering and context-modeling policies through a Meta Policy Generator, and applies Deficit-Aware Sampling to prioritize underrepresented morphology–context pairs before compositing defects via Poisson blending. Across four industrial datasets, AROMA's ROI selection achieved consistently higher morphology, context, and rare-pair coverage than random placement, and the full-image FID comparison confirmed that copy-paste synthesis preserves defect appearance fidelity while AROMA's advantage resides in contextual placement. Downstream anomaly detection results demonstrate that AROMA outperforms both random augmentation and the real-only baseline in Image AUROC, achieving an average Image AUROC of 0.923 across four datasets and four detector architectures. The principal contribution of this work is data-driven complexity policy learning, which eliminates the per-domain handcrafted re-engineering required by the predecessor CASDA framework and enables a single pipeline to generalize across heterogeneous industrial domains. Consistent with prior findings, augmentation benefit is shown to be architecture-dependent, and this sensitivity is reported transparently. Future work should pursue multi-seed statistical evaluation, extend validation to additional industrial domains, and integrate the proposed placement policy with generative models to combine context-aware placement with synthesized appearance diversity.
