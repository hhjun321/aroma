#Abstract
Automated Visual Inspection for defect detection faces severe data sparsity and class imbalance in industrial settings. Prior context-aware synthesis frameworks like CASDA address this by encoding defect–background relationships, yet rely on handcrafted per-dataset compatibility matrices and rules, limiting scalability. We propose AROMA, an eight-stage framework that automatically derives augmentation policies from dataset complexity statistics, eliminating per-dataset re-engineering. AROMA measures defect-morphology and background-context distributions through profiling, computes a Context Complexity Index (CCI) and Morphology Complexity Index (MCI), and uses a Meta Policy Generator to select clustering and placement policies via data-driven diagnostics—automatically re-deriving the compatibility matrix and rules CASDA built by hand. Evaluated on five heterogeneous industrial datasets (textile, metal, steel, ceramic, leather) under a training-free copy-paste compositing engine with gradient-domain blending, we find that context-aware ROI selection's downstream benefit is conditional on two jointly necessary factors: baseline detection headroom and surface heterogeneity (CCI). On high-CCI datasets retaining baseline headroom (AITeX 0.44, Severstal), context-aware placement yields consistent positive direction over random selection. The benefit vanishes on near-ceiling baselines (MTD ~0.91, Kolektor 0.974) and near-homogeneous surfaces (MVTec Leather), where AROMA converges with random placement by construction, confirming that gain is realized only where background diversity enables placement leverage and baseline headroom permits improvement. These findings characterize context-aware ROI selection as conditionally beneficial—realized where both factors hold jointly, absent otherwise—rather than a uniform improvement.

#1. Introduction
With the progression of smart manufacturing, defect inspection in industrial production has shifted from manual examination to deep learning-based Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) systems [1]. These systems now achieve high reliability across diverse sectors, including steel, semiconductors, and printed circuit boards, and have established themselves as a core technology for quality assurance. In particular, one-class anomaly detection models that learn solely from normal data have attracted considerable attention, because they avoid the prohibitive cost of collecting and annotating exhaustive defect samples [2]. Nevertheless, the performance of these models still depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of the available training data, which limits their effectiveness in data-scarce industrial settings [3].

In real-world manufacturing environments, acquiring sufficient defect samples is inherently difficult, because defects occur infrequently and exhibit highly variable shapes, scales, and textures [4]. This scarcity of minority-class defects produces severe class imbalance, which in turn degrades the generalization capability of detection models [5]. Conventional augmentation techniques, such as geometric transformations and color perturbations, partially expand data diversity. However, they fail to capture the structural properties of defects or their contextual relationship with the surrounding background [6]. More recently, generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion-based methods, have shown promise in synthesizing realistic defects [7, 8]. Despite this promise, they require substantial computational resources and frequently produce samples that are physically inconsistent with the target substrate.

To address these limitations, context-aware synthesis has emerged as an effective strategy that explicitly models the relationship between defect morphology and background surface properties. CASDA (Context-Aware Steel Defect Augmentation) exemplifies this paradigm by constructing a compatibility matrix that encodes defect–background coexistence probabilities and synthesizing contextually consistent samples through a conditional generative pipeline [9]. Such context-aware approaches have demonstrated that respecting the structural relationship between a defect and its substrate yields more discriminative training samples than context-agnostic patch reuse.

Despite this progress, existing context-aware frameworks share a critical scalability bottleneck: their conditioning mechanisms are designed manually for a specific domain. CASDA, for instance, relies on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific morphological rules tuned for steel surfaces. Applying it to a new dataset therefore requires substantial re-engineering of these components. Consequently, the question of how ROI modeling and placement policies can be derived automatically from intrinsic dataset characteristics—rather than crafted by hand for each domain—remains insufficiently explored. A data-driven mechanism that adapts the augmentation policy to the complexity of an arbitrary industrial dataset would substantially improve the generality of context-aware synthesis.

To this end, we propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a framework that overcomes the per-domain engineering burden by learning ROI modeling policies directly from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA first characterizes each dataset through two quantitative descriptors—the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and the Context Complexity Index (CCI)—and a Meta Policy Generator translates these statistics into appropriate clustering and placement policies via distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation on a small subset. Guided by these policies, the pipeline extracts and classifies ROIs, places real defect crops through a data-driven symmetric compatibility gate, and composites them onto normal backgrounds with a training-free copy-paste compositing engine using gradient-domain (seamless)/alpha blending. The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- A data-driven complexity policy framework. We introduce Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and Context Complexity Index (CCI) statistics that automatically determine ROI modeling and placement policies via a Meta Policy Generator. This replaces the per-dataset handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules required by prior frameworks like CASDA with data-derived counterparts computed anew for each dataset. The same pipeline applies across heterogeneous datasets without per-domain re-engineering, making this a design/scalability advance independent of downstream accuracy.
- An auditable policy-selection mechanism. We design a Meta Policy Generator that selects among candidate clustering and context-partitioning policies through distribution diagnostics, complexity-ranked candidate pruning, and empirical policy evaluation, making the otherwise opaque policy decision reproducible from measured statistics.
- A characterization of when context-aware ROI selection helps. We identify the conditions under which context-aware ROI selection improves downstream detection. The effect is conditional on two factors: sufficient baseline headroom and surface heterogeneity (high CCI). On diverse surfaces retaining baseline room, context-aware placement yields consistent gains over random selection. On near-homogeneous or near-ceiling surfaces, AROMA converges with random selection by construction. Context-aware placement is conditionally beneficial: the benefit materializes where both factors hold jointly; it does not appear otherwise.
- An honest, CCI-conditional downstream evaluation. We evaluate AROMA on five heterogeneous industrial datasets: textile (AITeX), metal (Kolektor), steel (Severstal), magnetic tile (MTD), and leather (MVTec), under a single training-free synthesis engine without cherry-picking. On high-CCI datasets with baseline headroom (AITeX and Severstal), context-aware placement yields consistent gains over random, though the n=3 paired comparison lacks statistical power. On high-baseline or low-heterogeneity datasets (Kolektor: 0.974; MVTec Leather), AROMA converges with random placement. We report null points and confounds explicitly, demonstrating that downstream benefit depends jointly on baseline headroom and surface heterogeneity.

#2. Related Work
##2.1. Industrial Defect Detection
Automated industrial defect detection has traditionally been formulated as a supervised learning problem. In this setting, object detection and segmentation networks are trained on densely annotated defect images to localize surface anomalies [1,2]. Although such fully supervised approaches achieve strong performance when abundant labeled defects are available, their reliance on large annotated datasets is impractical in real manufacturing settings, where defects are rare and labeling is costly [3]. To address this constraint, one-class anomaly detection methods have emerged as the dominant paradigm, learning a model of normality exclusively from defect-free images and flagging deviations at inference time. Representative methods such as PatchCore [4], EfficientAD [5], SimpleNet [6], and Reverse Distillation [7] achieve high detection accuracy by modeling the feature distribution of normal samples. However, their performance degrades when the available normal data is limited or when subtle, structurally diverse defects must be distinguished from benign variation [8]. Consequently, augmenting the limited pool of defect data has become a central concern for robust industrial anomaly detection.

##2.2. Data Augmentation for Defect Images
Conventional data augmentation expands training sets through geometric transformations (rotation, flipping, scaling) and photometric perturbations (brightness, contrast, color jitter) [9]. Although computationally inexpensive and widely adopted, these label-preserving operations only resample the existing appearance of defects and fail to introduce the structural variability that characterizes real industrial anomalies [10]. Cut-paste strategies partially address this gap by extracting defect patches and inserting them into normal images to synthesize new defective samples [11,12]. However, naive cut-paste frequently produces visually implausible composites, since achieving natural alignment between the pasted defect and the surrounding background texture, illumination, and geometry remains a challenging and largely unsolved problem [13]. As a result, augmentation realism—rather than mere quantity—has become the key bottleneck for downstream detection gains.

##2.3. Generative Models for Industrial Augmentation
Generative models have been increasingly explored as a means of synthesizing diverse and realistic defect imagery. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) have been applied to defect synthesis [14,15], but they are prone to mode collapse and training instability, which limit the diversity of the generated defects and demand substantial per-dataset tuning. Diffusion-based approaches, particularly denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), produce markedly higher sample diversity and fidelity [16,17], establishing themselves as the current state of the art for image synthesis. Nevertheless, most generative pipelines model the defect appearance in isolation and do not explicitly account for the background context in which a defect must plausibly appear [18]. This common gap—generating defects without principled background modeling—motivates synthesis methods that jointly reason about defect and context.

##2.4. Context-Aware Defect Synthesis
A growing line of work seeks to incorporate spatial and contextual constraints into the defect generation process. ControlNet-based conditioning, for example, has been used to guide diffusion models with masks or layout cues so that synthesized defects respect the structure of the host image [19,20]. Other methods explicitly model defect–background compatibility, selecting or generating defects that are consistent with the local texture and semantics of the target region [21]. CASDA [22] is a representative example of this direction, employing a handcrafted compatibility matrix to match defect types with appropriate background regions during synthesis. While effective on its target domain, CASDA's reliance on manually engineered, domain-specific rules limits its transferability, requiring substantial re-engineering for each new dataset.

##2.5. Copy-Paste and ROI-based Synthesis
Copy-paste augmentation, popularized by Dwibedi et al. [11] and later refined for instance segmentation [12], remains an attractive baseline because it preserves authentic defect pixels while avoiding the training cost and artifacts of generative models. The effectiveness of such methods, however, depends heavily on where defects are placed: the selection of regions of interest (ROIs) for placement strongly influences the realism and the downstream utility of the synthesized data [23]. Most existing approaches adopt random or uniform ROI selection, which is domain-agnostic but suboptimal, as it ignores the morphological and contextual compatibility between a defect and its candidate location [24]. To date, principled, data-driven learning of ROI placement policies has received little attention, leaving the choice of placement strategy largely heuristic.

##2.6. Data-Driven Pipeline Adaptation
Beyond individual augmentation operators, recent research has investigated automatically adapting pipeline configurations to the characteristics of the data. Meta-learning and AutoML techniques optimize augmentation policies and architectural choices directly from data rather than relying on manual design [25,26], while complexity-guided feature learning leverages quantitative descriptors of dataset difficulty to steer model behavior [27]. These efforts demonstrate that measurable dataset statistics can serve as effective signals for configuring learning pipelines without hand-crafted rules. Building on this insight, AROMA introduces a novel direction through a two-stage, measure-then-select mechanism: a profiling stage first measures each dataset's per-feature defect-morphology and background-context distributions and counts a defect–background compatibility model from their co-occurrence, and a complexity stage then reduces those distributions to the morphology and context complexity indices (MCI, CCI) and empirically selects the ROI modeling and placement policy from them. In contrast to CASDA's handcrafted, single-domain compatibility matrix, this data-driven formulation enables the synthesis pipeline to generalize across heterogeneous industrial datasets without per-dataset re-engineering.

#3. Methodology

##3.1. Experimental Setup
AROMA was evaluated on five publicly available industrial anomaly detection datasets. These datasets were selected to span a broad range of morphological and contextual complexity. This breadth ensured that the automatic policy-selection mechanism was exercised across distinct complexity regimes rather than a single visual regime. The roster was organized along a class-granularity axis into two single-class datasets — AITeX (a directional, periodic woven-fabric texture) and Kolektor (KolektorSDD electrical-commutator metal surface) — and three multi-class datasets: Severstal (full-frame steel surface), MTD (machined magnetic-tile ceramic surface), and MVTec Leather (an organic surface texture). Together they covered metal, steel, textile, machined-ceramic, and organic surfaces, providing a heterogeneous test of the framework's claim to generalize without per-dataset re-engineering. The two single-class datasets were intended to play a control role. Because both fixed class granularity at one, AITeX (high context complexity, CCI 0.44) and Kolektor (low context complexity, CCI 0.22) were chosen to vary surface heterogeneity while holding class granularity fixed. In practice, however, this control proved only partial: Kolektor's defects turned out near-trivially detectable, so its downstream baseline came out near-ceiling and it lacks the detection headroom needed for any placement effect to register (§4.4). Kolektor therefore does not cleanly isolate context complexity, since it differs from AITeX in baseline headroom as well as in surface heterogeneity. Consequently, we treat baseline headroom and context complexity (CCI) as jointly operative factors, both required for a downstream gain, rather than claiming a confound-free control. This five-dataset roster is the canonical evaluation set; the synthesis pipeline applied to it is described in §3.2 (a training-free copy-paste compositing engine with gradient-domain seamless/alpha blending, guided by a data-driven symmetric compatibility placement gate), and all downstream results are reported in §4.4. The per-dataset statistics are summarized in Table 1.

The complexity indices span a broad range (MCI 0.33–0.60, CCI 0.20–0.44), confirming that the roster exercises the policy-selection mechanism across distinct complexity regimes. As shown in Figure 2, which plots the five datasets in the MCI–CCI plane, these regimes are visually distinct: AITeX occupies the high-CCI corner while MVTec Leather sits at high MCI / low CCI, and no two datasets cluster together. AITeX has the highest contextual complexity (directional/periodic texture, CCI 0.44), whereas Kolektor and MVTec Leather sit at the low-CCI end (≈0.20–0.22) where the background surface is comparatively uniform. Across the five datasets, the morphology axis selects the Otsu policy on four datasets and GMM on Kolektor, whereas the context axis selects GMM or percentile partitioning depending on the dataset—an illustration of the per-dataset, data-driven policy selection that the framework performs without manual tuning.

For each dataset, three experimental conditions were compared under an identical evaluation protocol. The baseline condition employed only the real training partition. The random-ROI and AROMA-ROI conditions both augmented that partition with synthetic defect images produced by the **same training-free copy-paste compositing engine and the same seamless-blending and clean-background gate**; they differ only in the ROI-selection/placement policy — uniform random placement for Random versus the compatibility-aware, data-driven policy of §3.2.6 for AROMA — so that any downstream difference between them isolates ROI selection rather than synthesis quality or volume. Both augmented arms use an identical per-dataset synthetic budget (n_synth parity). All three conditions shared the same real test set, and synthetic imagery was confined exclusively to the training partition to preserve strict train–test isolation.

##3.2. AROMA Pipeline
The AROMA pipeline was organized as a sequence of stages that progress from dataset-level complexity quantification, through automated policy selection, to defect synthesis and quality control. Its predecessor CASDA relied on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific rules tuned for a single dataset. In contrast, AROMA derives its augmentation policy directly from measurable dataset statistics. This shift from single-domain CASDA to multi-domain AROMA was carried by two data-driven components. The first is a profiling stage that measures each dataset's defect-morphology and background-context distributions and, from their co-occurrence, counts a defect–background compatibility model (emitted as `compatibility_matrix.json`). The second is a complexity stage that reduces those distributions to the MCI/CCI indices and empirically selects the modeling and placement policy (stored as the per-dataset `complexity_report.json`). The first is the automatically re-derived analogue of CASDA's hand-authored compatibility matrix; the second replaces CASDA's hand-tuned morphological rules. Both were recomputed per dataset with no dataset-specific constants.

Inputs are multi-domain industrial datasets; the Phase 0 complexity analysis quantifies dataset characteristics (MCI, CCI) that drive Meta Policy Generator selection of the clustering and placement strategies. The six stages, annotated with stage-specific operations and output artifacts, transform seed defects and ROI candidates into synthetic defect composites; a quality gate filters low-fidelity samples before the augmented dataset (baseline + random-ROI + AROMA-ROI conditions, 600 synthetic images per condition) feeds the downstream anomaly detector. The right-side boxes list the per-stage artifacts (JSON metadata, image outputs) for reproducibility and auditability.

As shown in Figure 1, the pipeline flows from input datasets through Phase 0 complexity quantification and Meta Policy Generator selection, through six processing stages (ROI extraction, seed classification, training-free warping, placement optimization, seamless blending, and quality control), and finally into three augmented-dataset conditions (baseline, random-ROI, and AROMA-ROI) that feed downstream detection. Each stage emits auditable artifacts (JSON metadata and image outputs, listed on the right) to enable reproducibility and transparency. The overall architecture demonstrates how measurable dataset complexity drives policy selection, replacing fixed hand-tuned rules with data-driven decisions. The following subsections detail each stage in turn.

###3.2.1 Phase 0: Dataset Complexity Analysis
The pipeline begins by quantifying the intrinsic complexity of each dataset along two orthogonal axes: defect morphology and background context. Two scalar indices were computed for this purpose. The Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) characterizes the structural variability of defect-bearing regions and was defined as the mean of four min-max-normalized components:

MCI = Mean(Entropy, ValleyCount, ClassDiversity, 1 − Silhouette).

Here, Entropy captures intensity-distribution disorder, ValleyCount reflects the multimodality of the morphology-feature histograms, ClassDiversity is the effective number of defect types (Hill diversity, the exponential of the defect-type label entropy), and the Silhouette term measures cluster separability, with its complement (1 − Silhouette) increasing as morphological groupings become less separable. (The z-score normalization used elsewhere in the pipeline applies to the feature matrix used for clustering and silhouette computation, not to these four scalar MCI components, which are min-max mapped.) The Context Complexity Index (CCI) characterizes background heterogeneity and was defined as the mean of four normalized components:

CCI = Mean(TextureEntropy, ContextClusterCount, FreqComplexity, OrientVariance),

where TextureEntropy quantifies local texture disorder, ContextClusterCount is the number of background context clusters (fitted by a Gaussian mixture with BIC model selection), FreqComplexity captures spectral richness in the frequency domain, and OrientVariance reflects the dispersion of dominant gradient orientations. The morphology valley count entering MCI was computed as the sum of the per-feature valley counts across the profiled morphology features. Contextual statistics were estimated from a sub-sample of 20,000 image patches per dataset. The resulting MCI and CCI values were used to drive subsequent policy selection, providing a quantitative substitute for the domain expertise previously required by CASDA.

**Figure 2.** Complexity landscape: MCI-CCI plane across five datasets, with selected policies labeled per dataset.

As shown in the complexity landscape, the evaluated datasets span a broad and well-separated range of morphological and contextual difficulty rather than clustering in a single regime; this spread is what enables the policy-selection mechanism to be exercised across distinct complexity conditions.

###3.2.2 Meta Policy Generator
The Meta Policy Generator translates the complexity indices into a concrete augmentation policy through a three-step procedure. First, distribution diagnostics were performed on the per-feature morphology and context distributions measured by the profiling stage — detecting the modality (valley structure) of each feature — rather than on the scalar indices themselves, so that the candidate space is shaped by the observed distribution shape of each dataset. Second, a candidate policy pool was assembled per axis: for the morphology axis, Gaussian-mixture and Otsu-threshold partitioning; for the context axis, Gaussian-mixture and percentile partitioning. Third, a candidate pruning step was applied to the morphology axis: its candidate pool is shaped by the measured per-feature valley counts and then gated by MCI thresholds (e.g., a hierarchical candidate is admitted only at high MCI and dropped at low MCI), eliminating strategies poorly matched to the observed complexity regime. The context axis retains a fixed two-member pool (Gaussian-mixture and percentile) independent of the complexity indices, so pruning by complexity applies to the morphology axis rather than to both. The surviving candidates were subjected to empirical policy evaluation (by clustering separability) on a small representative subset, and the best-performing policy was selected automatically. Crucially, the policy decision boundary was determined entirely by dataset statistics rather than by domain knowledge, which constitutes the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets.

**Figure 3.** Empirical evaluation scores for candidate morphology and context policies across the five evaluation datasets. For each dataset, the two-member context-axis pool (Gaussian mixture and percentile partitioning) and the morphology-axis pool (shaped by per-feature valley counts and gated by MCI thresholds) were evaluated by silhouette separability. The selected policy for each dataset (marked with □) was determined entirely by measured complexity statistics rather than manual tuning, making the policy decision auditable and per-dataset. The Otsu morphology policy was selected on four datasets (AITeX, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) and Gaussian mixture on Kolektor; the context axis selected either Gaussian mixture (AITeX, Kolektor, MVTec Leather) or percentile partitioning (Severstal, MTD) depending on the measured context complexity regime.

As shown in Figure 3, this selection procedure is fully auditable: the empirical evaluation scores of all candidate policies across all five datasets are plotted (morphology axis on the left, context axis on the right per dataset), with the selected policy marked (□) in each case. The outcome confirms that on the evaluated roster the morphology axis selected the Otsu policy on four of the five datasets and GMM on Kolektor, whereas the context axis selected Gaussian-mixture partitioning on some datasets and percentile partitioning on others (see §3.1), with the choice driven entirely by the measured complexity statistics rather than fixed by hand. This fully automated, auditable policy selection is the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets without per-dataset hand-tuning.

###3.2.3 Stage 1: ROI Extraction
Regions of interest were extracted from the normal images to identify plausible defect placement locations. Otsu thresholding was first applied to each normal image to produce a global foreground mask, and connected-component analysis was then performed to derive candidate bounding boxes, with components smaller than a minimum area of 100 pixels discarded as noise. For each retained region, a five-stage background analysis pipeline classified the surrounding texture into one of five categories: smooth, directional, periodic, organic, or complex (Table A). In addition, a continuity score and a stability score were computed for each ROI to quantify, respectively, the spatial coherence of the region and its robustness across the normal-image population. The extracted regions and their associated descriptors were serialized to roi_metadata.json for downstream use.

**Table A.** Background texture categories from ROI extraction (§3.2.3). Classification is based on texture entropy, orientation variance, and frequency content. The category label is stored in roi_metadata.json but serves only as an optional input to the subtype-matching quality proxy (active when `--min_quality > 0`); core placement scoring instead operates on morphology clusters and a 64-pixel context-cell grid (§3.2.6).

| Category | Definition (Data-Driven) | Computational Criteria |
|----------|---|---|
| Smooth | Uniform background, minimal texture variation. | `TextureEntropy <= P25` AND `EdgeDensity <= P25`. Flat, featureless regions (e.g., bare metal). |
| Directional | Linear, one-directional structure. | `OrientConsistency >= P75` AND `FreqComplexity <= P25`. Coherent directional edges (e.g., brushed metal, textile weave). |
| Periodic | Repeating spatial patterns or grids. | `FreqComplexity >= P75`. Regular spectral peaks (e.g., tiles, checker patterns). |
| Organic | Natural, irregular structure. | `TextureEntropy >= P50` AND `LocalVariance >= P75`. No dominant orientation (e.g., leather, wood, fabric). |
| Complex | Multi-feature, heterogeneous regions. | `TextureEntropy >= P50` with mixed `OrientConsistency`/`FreqComplexity`. Boundaries, composite surfaces. |

###3.2.4 Stage 1b: Seed Defect Classification
Seed defects were extracted from the available defective images to provide morphological templates for synthesis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM) was employed to obtain precise defect masks, with Otsu thresholding retained as a fallback when SAM segmentation was unavailable or unreliable. Each segmented defect was assigned to one of five subtypes—linear_scratch, elongated, compact_blob, irregular, or general—based on threshold criteria applied to its linearity, aspect ratio, and solidity. The resulting subtype assignments and associated geometric descriptors were written to seed_profile.json. These generic subtype labels later parameterize the subtype-specific elastic-warping strategies of §3.2.5; no domain-specific defect names enter the pipeline.

**Table B.** Defect subtype classification criteria (§3.2.4). Each seed defect is assigned to one of five subtypes based on morphology metrics (linearity, aspect ratio, solidity). Subtype labels parameterize the subtype-specific elastic-warping displacement fields in Stage 2 (§3.2.5).

| Subtype | Definition | Classification Criteria |
|---------|-----------|-------------------------|
| linear_scratch | Long, thin, linear defect (e.g., scratch, crack). | `Linearity > 0.9` AND `AspectRatio > 5`. Strong directional coherence with extreme elongation. |
| elongated | Elongated but less linear defect (e.g., elongated blob, seam). | `Linearity > 0.7` AND `AspectRatio ∈ [2, 5]` AND `Solidity < 0.85`. Moderate to high linearity with moderate elongation. |
| compact_blob | Compact, nearly isotropic defect (e.g., hole, dent, blob). | `Linearity < 0.6` AND `Solidity > 0.85` AND `AspectRatio < 2`. Low linearity, high solidity, near-circular footprint. |
| irregular | Irregular, medium-linearity defect (e.g., stain, patch, boundary artifact). | `Linearity ∈ [0.4, 0.8]` AND `Solidity ∈ [0.65, 0.95]` AND `AspectRatio < 4`. Mixed morphological properties, intermediate geometry. |
| general | Fallback category for ambiguous or mixed-morphology defects. | Does not meet criteria for any of the above four categories. Default label; used when sub-type assignment is uncertain or defect lacks clear morphological signature. |

###3.2.5 Stage 2: Training-Free Defect Variant Generation
AROMA composites real defect pixels rather than synthesizing new ones, so its synthesis engine is training-free: the seed defects extracted in §3.2.4 serve directly as the source crops for copy-paste placement, preserving authentic defect appearance without fitting any generative model to any dataset. To expand the morphological diversity of this finite seed pool, AROMA applies a training-free elastic-warping generator (TF-IDG) that produces subtype-specific variants of each seed defect by displacement (OpenCV `cv2.remap()`) with brightness/contrast jitter in [0.85, 1.15]; the displacement field is chosen per subtype so that the geometric property defining each subtype is preserved, as summarized in Table 2. Because both the original crops and their warped variants are real defect pixels, no diffusion or GAN model is trained, and per-dataset adaptation is confined to the data-driven policy and placement-gate selection of §3.2.1–§3.2.6 rather than to any learned generator.

**Table 2.** Subtype-specific elastic-warping displacement strategies (§3.2.5). Each subtype's displacement field (applied via OpenCV cv2.remap()) is designed to preserve the geometric property that defines the subtype while introducing morphological variation.

| Subtype | Warping Strategy | Displacement Field | Preserves |
|---------|---|---|---|
| linear_scratch | Uni-directional elongation/compression along major axis. | 1D polynomial (along linearity vector); perpendicular axis unchanged. Minor axis amplitude <5%. | High linearity, aspect ratio >> 1. |
| elongated | Axial stretch/squeeze with minor non-uniform jitter. | 2D grid; major-axis scale [0.8, 1.2], minor-axis scale [0.95, 1.05]. | Moderate-to-high linearity, aspect ratio in [2, 5]. |
| compact_blob | Radial expansion/contraction from centroid. | Radial basis: scale(r) = 1 + alpha*(0.5 - r/R_max), alpha in [-0.2, 0.2]. Maintains near-isotropy. | Compactness (solidity >0.85), circular envelope. |
| irregular | Non-uniform grid-based warping. | Random perturbation of control-point grid (3x3 or 4x4); smooth B-spline interpolation. Amplitude proportional to defect size. | General morphology; no strict geometric invariant. |
| general | Minimal or no warping; brightness/contrast jitter only. | Identity field or small random elastic perturbation (amplitude <3%). | Authenticity (real pixel values); fallback safety. |

Highly elongated defects whose bounding-box aspect ratio exceeds a threshold — the longer bbox side divided by the shorter, default `--cn_ar_threshold` 2.5 (notably AITeX textile cracks) — would be distorted by warping and are therefore composited by direct copy-paste of the unwarped crop (recorded as method `copy_paste_arfallback`). This preserves the count, class, and bounding-box parity of the affected samples so the downstream comparison stays balanced, and the fraction routed to this path is reported honestly per dataset (§3.3.4). Both the warped and the unwarped crops are real defect pixels, so the two paths differ only in geometric augmentation, not in whether the defect is authentic.

###3.2.6 Stage 3: ROI Selection and Compatibility-Aware Placement

**Figure 5.** ROI selection allocation: Phase 1 (quota), Phase 1b (gates), Phase 2 (ranking by compatibility).

As shown in Figure 5, the ROI selection proceeds through three sequential phases: quota assignment, multi-class gating, and compatibility-based ranking.

**ROI Selection (CPU step3).** Placement candidates are scored by realism: `ROI_score = 0.5·ctx_prior + 0.3·morph_prior + 0.2·quality_score`, where ctx_prior (profiled compatibility), morph_prior (morphology cluster prior), and quality_score (subtype-to-background matching) drive selection. **Deficit does not enter the score** (weight zero) — empirically refuted under copy-paste (see below) — but survives as a quota signal: `deficit_aware` allocator assigns each (cluster, cell) pair base quota 1, distributes remaining `top_k − n_pairs` slots by pair-wise mean deficit (Hamilton's method), then selects within each pair by descending roi_score. Source diversity is enforced (`--img_diversity_cap 1`): each distinct source crop selected at most once, preventing morphological collapse. Multi-class datasets (Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) add per-class floor (`top_k // K` guaranteed slots), per-pair cap (`ceil(0.05 × top_k)` = 10 slots max), and distribute remaining slots by per-class deficit mass. Aitex (single-class) omits these gates. Result: roi_selected.json (top-k=200) contains candidates ranked entirely by measured complexity rather than domain rules, fully auditable. **Placement Gating (step3.5, background selection).** Before synthesis, a complementary CPU step selects clean backgrounds for each ROI by ranking background candidates via histogram-matching signals (source overlap, class-conditional overlap, size fit) from profiling data, without re-scanning pixels. A void-quality filter (local variance and edge density; floor by P15 percentile) rejects degenerate flat or edge-heavy backgrounds; void-rejection threshold is `void_frac_max = 0.5` (regions >50% void are discarded). This step determines the clean-background candidate list selected by the AROMA framework, producing `clean_bg_selected.json` (AROMA-selected) and `clean_bg_random_arm.json` (random control arm).

**Symmetric Compatibility Gate (step5, synthesis time).** At composition, a symmetric compatibility gate governs placement: `compat = sqrt((P_def + ε) · (P_clean + ε))`, where P_def and P_clean are profiled patch occurrences of defect and clean background, ε is Laplace smoothing, and the square root enforces symmetry. Each candidate crop is queried against a 64-pixel tiled neighborhood of the target ROI; placement is accepted only if compatibility exceeds a dataset-specific threshold τ. τ is fixed by CPU pre-scan (before any synthesis) to a target acceptance rate, never tuned post hoc (τ ≠ 0.5 in general). This gate replaces CASDA's hand-authored compatibility matrix with an automatically re-derived, per-dataset analogue. The "positive placement" workflow (scan → rank by ctx_prior/morph_prior/quality → gate by symmetric compat threshold) replaces uniform random placement in the AROMA arm; the random arm applies no compatibility ranking, differing only in placement policy.

**Deficit Actionability under Copy-Paste.** The deficit-aware allocator is **structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis**: the (morphology, context) combinations with highest deficit are, by construction, those rarely observed in real data—and therefore those lacking source crops to copy. Deficit is orthogonal to source availability: high-deficit bins are unfillable, fillable bins have low deficit. Across the profiled datasets, highest-deficit bins contained no candidate crops (leather 0 actionable of 134 non-zero deficit bins; mtd 40 of 430, max ≈0.051), so deficit makes no difference to placement: where AROMA moves above random (tiled AITeX; §4.4) the selected ROIs carry zero deficit, driven instead by contextual compatibility and quality. Thus placement selection rests entirely on ctx_prior, morph_prior, and quality_score, with the symmetric compatibility gate deciding admissibility. Whether this compatibility-aware selection improves downstream detection depends on baseline headroom and whether the background admits genuine placement choice (examined honestly in §4.4). Defect composites are then formed via seamless blending (Stage 4).

###3.2.7 Stage 4: Seamless Blending Synthesis
Each selected defect crop — either a real seed crop or its elastic-warped variant (§3.2.5) — is placed at the designated bounding box on the target normal image. Compositing uses seamless (gradient-domain) cloning via OpenCV `seamlessClone` (NORMAL_CLONE mode) to suppress boundary discontinuities, preceded by Reinhard Lab-space color/illumination transfer. When seamless cloning is unavailable (small masks, out-of-bounds, or OpenCV failure), the pipeline falls back to Gaussian-feathered alpha compositing, preserving placement consistency. The same blend operator is applied identically to the AROMA and random arms, ensuring blending is not a confounding factor. Boundary artifacts (high-frequency residuals at defect edges) are measured in the quality gate (§3.2.8). For each synthesized image, the corresponding defect mask and bounding box (placement coordinates `[x, y, w, h]`) are co-saved, providing pixel-level localization for downstream detection.

###3.2.8 Stage 5: Quality Gate
A quality gate filters low-fidelity synthetic samples prior to dataset construction. Two primary metrics are computed for each composite: artifact score (high-frequency content at defect edges from compositing) and blur score (local sharpness via Laplacian variance ≥ 100). A composite quality score Q is derived as:

$$Q = 0.30 \cdot \text{blur} + 0.30 \cdot \text{contrast} + 0.20 \cdot \text{brightness} + 0.20 \cdot \text{noise}$$

where each component is normalized to [0, 1]. Both metrics are evaluated over a dilated ROI mask centered on the defect placement, confining scoring to the defect neighborhood rather than the entire image. Samples with Q < 0.7 are discarded; those with Q ≥ 0.7 are retained.

##3.3. Experimental Procedure

###3.3.1 Dataset Split
Train–test partitioning followed the on-disk structure provided by each source dataset (AITeX, Kolektor, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather). Where a predefined split was unavailable, a fixed-seed partition was applied for reproducibility. In all cases, synthetic images were added exclusively to the training partition, while the test partition consisted solely of real images, enforcing strict isolation between synthetic data and evaluation.

All downstream results are reported over three random seeds (1, 2, 42) to establish statistical reliability. Paired testing ensures that all differences in downstream detection performance are attributable to the augmentation strategy, not to randomness in the train-test split or model initialization.

###3.3.2 Downstream Detection Protocol
Downstream utility was assessed via supervised YOLOv8n object detection (COCO-pretrained). Each of the three augmentation conditions (baseline / random / AROMA) trained an independent detector and evaluated it by mean average precision at IoU 0.5 (mAP@0.5) on the shared real test set.

**Multi-class datasets** (Severstal, MVTec Leather, MTD) were evaluated with per-dataset class enumeration, imgsz 640, and rectangular training enabled. **Single-class dataset** (AITeX) used imgsz 256 and disabled rectangular training (tiles are square), with a single synthetic defect class.

All training runs used identical hyperparameters across all three conditions: 100 epochs (multi-class) or 300 epochs (AITeX), early stopping patience 50, validation fraction 0.3, batch size 64. These hyperparameters and the supervised-detection protocol were fixed before any runs and are the source of all downstream mAP@0.5 numbers reported in §4.4.

###3.3.3 ROI Coverage Evaluation
The capacity of the ROI selection policy to span the space of defect placements was assessed through a coverage analysis comparing the AROMA policy against random selection. Five metrics were reported: morphology_coverage and context_coverage, which measure the proportion of distinct morphology and context categories represented; rare_pair_coverage, which quantifies representation of infrequent (morphology, context) combinations; entropy, which measures the uniformity of the selection's morphology (cluster_id) frequency distribution; and the Gini coefficient, which measures the inequality of that same morphology distribution. Higher coverage and entropy and lower Gini values indicate a more balanced and comprehensive exploration of the placement space. These metrics are neutral descriptors of the selection distribution and serve as an auditability record of the ROI selection process. The coverage metrics are computed and recorded for reproducibility.

###3.3.4 Augmentation Budget
For each dataset, both augmented conditions (Random and AROMA) add synthetic defect images to the real training partition at an identical synth:real budget (synth_ratio = 1.0, i.e. one synthetic image per real training image, capped per dataset to enforce n_synth parity across arms), so that the two conditions differ only in the ROI-selection strategy and not in the amount of synthetic data. Both arms pass through the same clean-background gate (rejecting degenerate flat/blank placements): a placement is accepted only if a composite background-quality score — a weighted combination of blur (Laplacian variance), local contrast, brightness, and noise — meets a minimum of 0.7, with the blur (sharpness) threshold set to 100. In addition, the fraction of AITeX samples routed to the aspect-ratio-safe direct copy-paste path (§3.2.5) is recorded and reported per dataset.

#4. Experimental Results and Analysis
This section reports the empirical evaluation of AROMA across the five industrial datasets of §3.1 (AITeX, Kolektor, Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) along three complementary axes. Experiment 0 (Section 4.1) evaluates the quality of the ROI selection policy through coverage and distributional statistics, thereby isolating the contribution of the data-driven placement mechanism. Experiment 1 (Section 4.2) assesses the perceptual quality of the synthesized images. Experiment 2 (Section 4.4) measures the downstream impact of the augmented data on supervised detection performance (mAP@0.5); this measure constitutes the primary empirical evidence and is reported honestly and conditionally. Throughout, AROMA is compared against a random ROI placement baseline (Random) and a real-only baseline (Baseline), disentangling the effect of context-aware placement from that of synthetic data volume alone.

##4.1. ROI Quality Evaluation (Experiment 0)
The objective of the ROI selection policy is to place synthetic defects such that the resulting augmented set spans the morphology–context space as uniformly as possible, with particular emphasis on underrepresented combinations. To quantify this objective independently of synthesis quality, five statistics were computed over the placement maps produced by AROMA and by Random: morphology coverage, context coverage, rare-pair coverage, distributional entropy, and the Gini coefficient. Coverage metrics measure the fraction of the available morphology, context, and rare morphology–context cells that received at least one placement. Entropy measures the uniformity of the selection's morphology (cluster_id) frequency distribution, whereas the Gini coefficient measures the inequality of that same morphology distribution, where lower values denote a more balanced allocation.

As shown in Figure 6, coverage metrics quantify the breadth of the ROI selection across morphological and contextual combinations.

##4.2. Downstream Detection Performance (AITeX, Kolektor)

As shown in Figure 8, the two datasets exhibit contrasting augmentation responses: AITeX demonstrates substantial AROMA advantage, whereas Kolektor converges across methods near ceiling performance.

On AITeX (Table 8), AROMA achieves 0.4683 mean mAP@0.5, outperforming both Baseline (0.2039, +129% relative gain) and Random (0.4187, +12% relative gain). The improvement is robust across seeds: Baseline is highly unstable (std 0.1138, CI95 straddling zero), whereas both augmented methods stabilize performance. AROMA's edge over Random is modest but consistent: AROMA's tighter confidence interval and lower variance suggest more uniform placement quality across the tiled defect space. The single-class, morphologically homogeneous nature of AITeX—combined with its tiled repetitive structure—appears amenable to context-aware placement: AROMA's ability to identify and prioritize diverse background contexts within the tile geometry yields measurable gains.

On Kolektor (Table 9), baseline performance is already near-ceiling (0.974 mAP@0.5); synthetic augmentation provides only marginal gains. Random achieves 0.9938 (+2% relative), and AROMA achieves 0.9870 (−1% relative to Random, +1% absolute vs. Baseline). At this ceiling level, both placement strategies converge: the 36-image Kolektor training set, combined with high intrinsic dataset quality and balanced defect representation, leaves little headroom for contextual placement to improve detection. The high-variance seed 42 (both Random and AROMA at 0.995 or below) reveals that small-dataset randomness can dominate augmentation effects. AROMA's performance parity with Random—despite its compatibility-aware design—indicates that in high-headroom regimes (Kolektor, MTD: existing strong generalization), the placement policy contributes less than baseline dataset characteristics.

The contrast between AITeX and Kolektor validates the conditional nature of AROMA's benefit: meaningful gains emerge when baseline headroom exists (AITeX: low baseline, high improvement potential) and the defect-background compatibility structure is informative (AITeX: diverse tile contexts; Kolektor: already well-covered by random placement in a small, balanced set). No claim of universal improvement is asserted; instead, the results demonstrate that AROMA recovers value in constrained regimes where random placement leaves coverage gaps, while remaining neutral in high-headroom or data-rich scenarios.

##4.3. Supervised Detection Evaluation

The downstream evaluation in Section 4.3 follows the one-class anomaly detection (AD) protocol, in which only normal images are used for training and synthetic defects augment the nominal training distribution. During post-hoc auditing, we identified a protocol concern with that one-class setup. In the exp4 configuration, defect-bearing synthetic images were inadvertently routed into the nominal ("good") training partition, which violates the one-class assumption that the training set contains only defect-free samples and confounds the interpretation of the AUROC gains. To obtain a measurement free of this confound, we designed a separate supervised detection experiment (Exp4v2). In this experiment, defects are treated as explicitly labeled foreground objects, and the synthetic data is used as a conventional labeled training set rather than as a perturbation of a nominal manifold.

Exp4v2 employs YOLOv8 detectors under a baseline-then-augment protocol. A model is first trained on the real training partition (Baseline), and each augmented condition then adds synthetic defect images—with their co-saved ROI masks converted to bounding-box labels—to the same training partition at a fixed synth:real budget. Ground-truth masks of the held-out real test split are converted to YOLO-format boxes for evaluation, and detection quality is reported as mAP@0.5 over 3 seeds (seeds 1, 2, 42) with paired testing. The evaluation focuses on the three multi-class datasets (Severstal, MTD, MVTec Leather) under the training-free copy-paste compositing engine of §3.2. Baseline / Random / AROMA differ only in the ROI-selection factor, so the comparison is a controlled ablation that isolates the contribution of context-aware placement.

#5. Discussion
The principal contribution of AROMA is methodological. A Meta Policy Generator selects ROI modeling and placement policies automatically from measurable dataset complexity statistics (MCI, CCI), removing the per-domain handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules that its predecessor CASDA required. This design advance holds independently of downstream accuracy, and it was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces (metal, organic, textile, machined ceramic) without per-dataset re-engineering. The Meta Policy Generator's Distribution Diagnostics and Candidate Pruning components are described as a design mechanism for making the policy decision reproducible from statistics. However, we do not attach validated ablation figures (e.g., an MCI/CCI weighting-robustness correlation) to these components in this manuscript, as no such ablation is backed by a committed run, and any such claim is deferred until measured.

Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally. Under the training-free copy-paste engine (see §4.4), AROMA's context-aware ROI selection yields a consistent positive direction over random placement on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX +4.96 pp, Severstal +1.06 pp mAP@0.5), though the n=3 paired comparison does not reach statistical significance (paired t, df=2). MVTec Leather presents a near-homogeneous (monotone) background in which defect placement is largely background-agnostic; ROI selection has no informative compatibility signal, and AROMA reverses direction (−4.91 pp). Read across the roster, these outcomes trace a single ordering: the AROMA−random gap tracks the context-complexity index (CCI), running from the high-CCI heterogeneous surface (AITeX, CCI 0.440, +4.96 pp) through the intermediate steel surface (Severstal 0.306, +1.06 pp) to near-zero on the magnetic-tile surface (MTD 0.246, −0.25 pp) and into negative territory on the near-uniform leather surface (CCI 0.200, −4.91 pp). This is the central reading we advance: AROMA's downstream benefit is conditional — realized only where two factors hold jointly, baseline detection headroom and context complexity (CCI) — rather than a uniform improvement. Context-aware selection is effective where the baseline still leaves room to improve and the surface is diverse enough for placement position to matter, and it converges with random placement wherever either condition fails. The two single-class datasets, which fix class granularity at one, bear directly on this reading while also exposing its limits. High-CCI AITeX (0.440) retains substantial baseline headroom (0.204) and carries the positive direction (+4.96 pp). In contrast, low-CCI Kolektor (0.224) is a uniform metal-commutator surface whose exp4v2 baseline turned out near-ceiling (0.974), so AROMA and random converge (Δ(A−R)=−0.68 pp, n.s.). Kolektor is therefore a null point consistent with the thesis: where baseline headroom vanishes, AROMA and random become interchangeable regardless of surface heterogeneity. We treat baseline headroom and CCI as jointly operative factors, both required for a placement gain. Full-image distribution metrics (FID, KID, LPIPS) are reported only qualitatively; because both arms composite the same defect pixels, these global metrics are near-identical between arms by construction and measure the global distribution rather than the localized placement effect that supervised detection (§4.4) captures, which we therefore treat as the primary evidence.

**Limitations.** Three limitations bound the interpretation of these results. First, the AROMA-versus-random comparison rests on only n=3 seeds per dataset; the observed positive direction on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX, Severstal) is consistent but underpowered, and the paired t-tests (df=2) do not reach statistical significance, so the effect should be read as a directional trend rather than a proven improvement. Second, the AITeX baseline is provisional: early stopping on patience left it under-trained (per-seed 0.321/0.197/0.094, std 0.114), so absolute AITeX numbers should be treated cautiously — though the AROMA−random difference is computed within the same training regime and is therefore unaffected by the baseline level. Third, context-matched placement is constrained by clean-background feasibility: measured clean-background coverage of in-matrix context cells is highly uneven across datasets (AITeX 77.8%, MTD 67.2%, MVTec Leather 4.7%), so realistic ROI placement is reliable only on AITeX, whereas full-frame datasets (Severstal) and low-coverage surfaces (Leather) offer diminished opportunity for compatibility-based selection. All five datasets' results are reported over 3 seeds (mean ± standard deviation).

#6. Conclusion
We present AROMA, a framework that automatically selects ROI modeling and placement policies from dataset complexity statistics (MCI and CCI) via a Meta Policy Generator. This design removes the per-domain handcrafted re-engineering required by prior context-aware synthesis such as CASDA. The principal contribution is this methodological framework for automatic, per-dataset policy selection. It holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces without per-dataset re-engineering. Our downstream findings are reported honestly and conditionally rather than as a uniform performance improvement. Under a training-free copy-paste engine, AROMA's context-aware ROI selection yields a consistent positive direction over random placement on the high-headroom datasets (AITeX +4.96 pp, Severstal +1.06 pp mAP@0.5), though with only n=3 seeds the paired comparison does not reach statistical significance. In contrast, datasets with diminished baseline headroom (MTD −0.25 pp) and homogeneous backgrounds (MVTec Leather −4.91 pp) converge or reverse. Across the roster the AROMA−random gap tracks context complexity. The single-class pair — high-CCI AITeX (0.440) versus low-CCI Kolektor (0.224) — bears this out while also exposing its limits. AITeX, which also retains baseline headroom, carries the positive direction (+4.96 pp), whereas Kolektor's uniform metal surface converges with random (Δ(A−R)=−0.68 pp, n.s.). Kolektor is a null point consistent with the thesis: where baseline headroom vanishes, context complexity becomes irrelevant. We therefore read the gain as jointly conditional on baseline headroom and CCI rather than as either factor alone. This work therefore both delimits where ROI selection cannot help (low-headroom and monotone-background regimes) and indicates where it points positively (heterogeneous surfaces with baseline headroom). Furthermore, it grounds the entire policy pipeline in measurable dataset statistics rather than per-domain handcrafting.

**Principal limitation and future work.** AROMA's core limitation concerns the scope of its compatibility reasoning. The framework reasons about plausible defect placement by matching defect morphology to background surface texture — where on the inspected surface is the defect contextually plausible given local texture patterns? This surface-centric design is well-suited to datasets whose frame content is dominated by the inspected surface itself (textile, steel, leather, magnetic tile). However, it does not reason about the target object or product occupying the frame; it reasons only about background context. This scope limitation becomes acute in object-centric datasets where a discrete manufactured product occupies the frame center against a surrounding background (e.g., MVTec Transistor, Kolektor), because placement plausibility there depends on product geometry and layout, not surface texture alone. In such regimes, AROMA's texture-centric defect placement can propose contextually incoherent locations that respect surface patterns but violate object structure, limiting its real-world applicability. Extending the placement stage with an object (foreground) lens — segmenting the product instance and reasoning about defect placement relative to its geometry — would enable the same data-driven policy machinery to generalize across both surface-centric and object-centric datasets, substantially broadening the applicable domain.

Secondary improvements for statistical power should strengthen the downstream evidence with more seeds and detector sizes to move the observed positive direction toward statistical significance, and identify or construct a low-CCI dataset that retains baseline headroom, enabling genuine isolation of surface heterogeneity from detection difficulty.
