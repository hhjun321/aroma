#Abstract
Anomaly detection in industrial Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) is constrained by severe data sparsity and class imbalance, as defect samples are rare and morphologically heterogeneous in real production environments. Prior context-aware synthesis frameworks, such as CASDA, mitigate this scarcity by encoding defect–background relationships, but rely on domain-specific handcrafted rules and a manually constructed compatibility matrix that must be re-engineered for each new dataset, limiting their scalability. We propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), an eight-stage framework whose primary contribution is methodological: it automatically selects ROI modeling policies from dataset complexity statistics, eliminating the per-dataset re-engineering required by handcrafted approaches. From an automated profiling stage that measures each dataset's defect-morphology and background-context distributions and counts a defect–background compatibility model from their co-occurrence, AROMA computes a Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and a Context Complexity Index (CCI), which a Meta Policy Generator translates into clustering and placement policies through distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation. Together these two data-driven components re-derive automatically, for every dataset, the two artifacts CASDA built by hand — its compatibility matrix and its morphological rules. Alongside this framework, we report an engine-dependent downstream finding and the mechanism behind it. Under a trained ControlNet + seamless-blending generator — the primary AROMA synthesis engine — context-aware ROI selection yields its largest and only statistically consistent gain on the hardest, highest-headroom dataset (Severstal, +0.096 mAP@0.5 over random placement, consistent across all three seeds), whereas near-ceiling datasets converge with random augmentation. Crucially, the *same* selection policy is statistically indistinguishable from random under a training-free copy-paste engine, including on Severstal, which is null there. We explain this contrast mechanistically: deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting is structurally inert under copy-paste because the highest-deficit (morphology, context) combinations are precisely those for which no source patch exists to copy (deficit is orthogonal to source availability), so context-aware selection can only pay off once a generative engine is able to synthesize the targeted combinations rather than relocate existing ones. The Severstal result confirms this prediction. [Quantitative results are reported only in §4.4 from committed result files; earlier projected numbers, including a previously stated "Image AUROC of 0.923," were unbacked and have been permanently removed.]

#1. Introduction
With the progression of smart manufacturing, defect inspection in industrial production has shifted from manual examination to deep learning-based Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) systems [1]. These systems now achieve high reliability across diverse sectors, including steel, semiconductors, and printed circuit boards, and have established themselves as a core technology for quality assurance. In particular, one-class anomaly detection models that learn solely from normal data have attracted considerable attention, as they avoid the prohibitive cost of collecting and annotating exhaustive defect samples [2]. Nevertheless, the performance of these models still depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of the available training data, which limits their effectiveness in data-scarce industrial settings [3].

In real-world manufacturing environments, acquiring sufficient defect samples is inherently difficult because defects occur infrequently and exhibit highly variable shapes, scales, and textures [4]. This scarcity of minority-class defects produces severe class imbalance, which degrades the generalization capability of detection models [5]. Conventional augmentation techniques, such as geometric transformations and color perturbations, partially expand data diversity but fail to capture the structural properties of defects or their contextual relationship with the surrounding background [6]. More recently, generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion-based methods, have shown promise in synthesizing realistic defects [7, 8]; however, they require substantial computational resources and frequently produce samples that are physically inconsistent with the target substrate.

To address these limitations, context-aware synthesis has emerged as an effective strategy that explicitly models the relationship between defect morphology and background surface properties. CASDA (Context-Aware Steel Defect Augmentation) exemplifies this paradigm by constructing a compatibility matrix that encodes defect–background coexistence probabilities and synthesizing contextually consistent samples through a conditional generative pipeline [9]. Such context-aware approaches have demonstrated that respecting the structural relationship between a defect and its substrate yields more discriminative training samples than context-agnostic patch reuse.

Despite this progress, existing context-aware frameworks share a critical scalability bottleneck: their conditioning mechanisms are designed manually for a specific domain. CASDA, for instance, relies on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific morphological rules tuned for steel surfaces, and applying it to a new dataset requires substantial re-engineering of these components. Consequently, the question of how ROI modeling and placement policies can be derived automatically from intrinsic dataset characteristics—rather than crafted by hand for each domain—remains insufficiently explored. A data-driven mechanism that adapts the augmentation policy to the complexity of an arbitrary industrial dataset would substantially improve the generality of context-aware synthesis.

To this end, we propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a framework that overcomes the per-domain engineering burden by learning ROI modeling policies directly from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA first characterizes each dataset through two quantitative descriptors—the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and the Context Complexity Index (CCI)—and a Meta Policy Generator translates these statistics into appropriate clustering and placement policies via distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation on a small subset. Guided by these policies, the pipeline extracts and classifies ROIs, synthesizes defect appearance with a per-dataset fine-tuned ControlNet generator (with a training-free elastic-warping fallback for aspect-ratio-unsafe cases), places defects through a data-driven symmetric compatibility gate, and composites them onto normal backgrounds using seamless (gradient-domain) blending. The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- **A data-driven complexity policy framework.** We introduce MCI and CCI statistics that, through a Meta Policy Generator, automatically determine ROI modeling and placement policies, replacing the per-dataset handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules required by prior context-aware frameworks such as CASDA with data-derived counterparts computed anew for each dataset — a compatibility model counted from that dataset's own defect–background co-occurrence and modeling policies chosen from its measured complexity statistics. This contribution is a design/scalability advance and is independent of downstream accuracy: the same pipeline is applied across heterogeneous datasets without per-domain re-engineering.
- **An auditable policy-selection mechanism.** We design a Meta Policy Generator that selects among candidate clustering and context-partitioning policies through distribution diagnostics, complexity-ranked candidate pruning, and empirical policy evaluation, making the otherwise opaque policy decision reproducible from measured statistics.
- **A mechanistic account of when ROI selection helps, and why it needs a generative engine.** We demonstrate that deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis: the highest-deficit (morphology, context) combinations are those with no real source crop to copy, so deficit is orthogonal to source availability, and under copy-paste AROMA is statistically indistinguishable from random. Replacing the engine with a trained ControlNet generator that can *synthesize* the targeted combinations removes this limitation, and the prediction is confirmed downstream (below). This clarifies that context/deficit-aware ROI selection requires a generative engine rather than patch reuse.
- **An honest, engine- and headroom-conditional downstream evaluation.** We evaluate AROMA across four heterogeneous industrial datasets under both engines, without cherry-picking. Under the ControlNet engine, context-aware selection produces its largest and only all-seed-consistent gain on the hardest, highest-headroom dataset (Severstal, +0.096 mAP@0.5 over random), exactly where the identical policy was null under copy-paste; near-ceiling datasets (MTD, MVTec Leather) converge with random augmentation, and we analyze the ceiling and measurement confounds that bound the result. This is a conditional positive tied to baseline headroom and a generative engine, not a uniform improvement.

#2. Related Work
##2.1. Industrial Defect Detection
Automated industrial defect detection has traditionally been formulated as a supervised learning problem, in which object detection and segmentation networks are trained on densely annotated defect images to localize surface anomalies [1,2]. While such fully supervised approaches achieve strong performance when abundant labeled defects are available, their reliance on large annotated datasets is impractical in real manufacturing settings, where defects are rare and labeling is costly [3]. To address this constraint, one-class anomaly detection methods have emerged as the dominant paradigm, learning a model of normality exclusively from defect-free images and flagging deviations at inference time. Representative methods such as PatchCore [4], EfficientAD [5], SimpleNet [6], and Reverse Distillation [7] achieve high detection accuracy by modeling the feature distribution of normal samples, yet their performance degrades when the available normal data is limited or when subtle, structurally diverse defects must be distinguished from benign variation [8]. Consequently, augmenting the limited pool of defect data has become a central concern for robust industrial anomaly detection.

##2.2. Data Augmentation for Defect Images
Conventional data augmentation expands training sets through geometric transformations (rotation, flipping, scaling) and photometric perturbations (brightness, contrast, color jitter) [9]. Although computationally inexpensive and widely adopted, these label-preserving operations only resample the existing appearance of defects and fail to introduce the structural variability that characterizes real industrial anomalies [10]. Cut-paste strategies partially address this gap by extracting defect patches and inserting them into normal images to synthesize new defective samples [11,12]. However, naive cut-paste frequently produces visually implausible composites, since achieving natural alignment between the pasted defect and the surrounding background texture, illumination, and geometry remains a challenging and largely unsolved problem [13]. As a result, augmentation realism—rather than mere quantity—has become the key bottleneck for downstream detection gains.

##2.3. Generative Models for Industrial Augmentation
Generative models have been increasingly explored as a means of synthesizing diverse and realistic defect imagery. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) have been applied to defect synthesis [14,15], but they are prone to mode collapse and training instability, which limit the diversity of the generated defects and demand substantial per-dataset tuning. Diffusion-based approaches, particularly denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), produce markedly higher sample diversity and fidelity [16,17], establishing themselves as the current state of the art for image synthesis. Nevertheless, most generative pipelines model the defect appearance in isolation and do not explicitly account for the background context in which a defect must plausibly appear [18]. This common gap—generating defects without principled background modeling—motivates synthesis methods that jointly reason about defect and context.

##2.4. Context-Aware Defect Synthesis
A growing line of work seeks to incorporate spatial and contextual constraints into the defect generation process. ControlNet-based conditioning, for example, has been used to guide diffusion models with masks or layout cues so that synthesized defects respect the structure of the host image [19,20]. Other methods explicitly model defect–background compatibility, selecting or generating defects that are consistent with the local texture and semantics of the target region [21]. CASDA [22] is a representative example of this direction, employing a handcrafted compatibility matrix to match defect types with appropriate background regions during synthesis. While effective on its target domain, CASDA's reliance on manually engineered, domain-specific rules limits its transferability, requiring substantial re-engineering for each new dataset.

##2.5. Copy-Paste and ROI-based Synthesis
Copy-paste augmentation, popularized by Dwibedi et al. [11] and later refined for instance segmentation [12], remains an attractive baseline because it preserves authentic defect pixels while avoiding the training cost and artifacts of generative models. The effectiveness of such methods, however, depends heavily on where defects are placed: the selection of regions of interest (ROIs) for placement strongly influences the realism and the downstream utility of the synthesized data [23]. Most existing approaches adopt random or uniform ROI selection, which is domain-agnostic but suboptimal, as it ignores the morphological and contextual compatibility between a defect and its candidate location [24]. To date, principled, data-driven learning of ROI placement policies has received little attention, leaving the choice of placement strategy largely heuristic.

##2.6. Data-Driven Pipeline Adaptation
Beyond individual augmentation operators, recent research has investigated automatically adapting pipeline configurations to the characteristics of the data. Meta-learning and AutoML techniques optimize augmentation policies and architectural choices directly from data rather than relying on manual design [25,26], while complexity-guided feature learning leverages quantitative descriptors of dataset difficulty to steer model behavior [27]. These efforts demonstrate that measurable dataset statistics can serve as effective signals for configuring learning pipelines without hand-crafted rules. Building on this insight, AROMA introduces a novel direction through a two-stage, measure-then-select mechanism: a profiling stage first measures each dataset's per-feature defect-morphology and background-context distributions and counts a defect–background compatibility model from their co-occurrence, and a complexity stage then reduces those distributions to the morphology and context complexity indices (MCI, CCI) and empirically selects the ROI modeling and placement policy from them. In contrast to CASDA's handcrafted, single-domain compatibility matrix, this data-driven formulation enables the synthesis pipeline to generalize across heterogeneous industrial datasets without per-dataset re-engineering.

#3. Methodology

##3.1. Experimental Setup
AROMA was evaluated on publicly available industrial anomaly detection datasets selected to span a broad range of morphological and contextual complexity, so that the automatic policy-selection mechanism is exercised across distinct complexity regimes rather than a single visual regime. The evaluated datasets are Severstal (full-frame steel surface, 1600×256 px, four defect classes with large foreground extent), MVTec Leather (an organic surface texture, five defect classes), AITeX (a directional, periodic woven-fabric texture; the raw 4096×256 px strips have a 16:1 aspect ratio that collapses under a square-letterbox detector, so AITeX is evaluated under a tiled protocol — 256×256 tiles at stride 128, i.e. 50% overlap, as a single defect class), and MTD (machined magnetic-tile ceramic surface, five defect classes). Together they cover metal, organic, textile, and machined-ceramic surfaces, providing a heterogeneous test of the framework's claim to generalize without per-dataset re-engineering. This four-dataset roster is the canonical evaluation set; the synthesis pipeline applied to it is described in §3.2 (a trained ControlNet + seamless-blending generator as the primary engine, with a training-free copy-paste engine retained as an ablation), and all downstream results are reported in §4.4. For each dataset, three experimental conditions were compared under an identical evaluation protocol. The baseline condition employed only the real training partition. Both augmented arms use an identical per-dataset synthetic budget (n_synth parity). All three conditions shared the same real test set, and synthetic imagery was confined exclusively to the training partition to preserve strict train–test isolation.

##3.2. AROMA Pipeline
The AROMA pipeline is organized as a sequence of stages that progress from dataset-level complexity quantification through automated policy selection to defect synthesis and quality control. In contrast to its predecessor CASDA, which relied on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific rules tuned for a single dataset, AROMA derives its augmentation policy directly from measurable dataset statistics. This shift from single-domain CASDA to multi-domain AROMA is carried by two data-driven components: a profiling stage that measures each dataset's defect-morphology and background-context distributions and, from their co-occurrence, counts a defect–background compatibility model (emitted as `compatibility_matrix.json`), and a complexity stage that reduces those distributions to the MCI/CCI indices and empirically selects the modeling and placement policy (committed as the per-dataset `complexity_report.json`). The first is the automatically re-derived analogue of CASDA's hand-authored compatibility matrix; the second replaces CASDA's hand-tuned morphological rules — both recomputed per dataset with no dataset-specific constants. The overall architecture and the data flow between the eight stages are illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. Each stage is detailed in the following subsections.

###3.2.1 Phase 0: Dataset Complexity Analysis
The pipeline begins by quantifying the intrinsic complexity of each dataset along two orthogonal axes: defect morphology and background context. Two scalar indices were computed for this purpose. The Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) characterizes the structural variability of defect-bearing regions and was defined as the mean of four min-max-normalized components:

MCI = Mean(Entropy, ValleyCount, ClassDiversity, 1 − Silhouette).

Here, Entropy captures intensity-distribution disorder, ValleyCount reflects the multimodality of the morphology-feature histograms, ClassDiversity is the effective number of defect types (Hill diversity, the exponential of the defect-type label entropy), and the Silhouette term measures cluster separability, with its complement (1 − Silhouette) increasing as morphological groupings become less separable. (The z-score normalization used elsewhere in the pipeline applies to the feature matrix used for clustering and silhouette computation, not to these four scalar MCI components, which are min-max mapped.) The Context Complexity Index (CCI) characterizes background heterogeneity and was defined as the mean of four normalized components:

CCI = Mean(TextureEntropy, ContextClusterCount, FreqComplexity, OrientVariance),

where TextureEntropy quantifies local texture disorder, ContextClusterCount is the number of background context clusters (fitted by a Gaussian mixture with BIC model selection), FreqComplexity captures spectral richness in the frequency domain, and OrientVariance reflects the dispersion of dominant gradient orientations. The morphology valley count entering MCI was computed as the sum of the per-feature valley counts across the profiled morphology features, and contextual statistics were estimated from a sub-sample of 20,000 image patches per dataset. The resulting MCI and CCI values were used to drive subsequent policy selection, providing a quantitative substitute for the domain expertise previously required by CASDA. Figure~\ref{fig:complexity_landscape} positions the four evaluated datasets in the MCI–CCI complexity plane, confirming that the selected datasets span a broad and well-separated range of morphological and contextual difficulty rather than clustering in a single regime; this spread is what enables the policy-selection mechanism to be exercised across distinct complexity conditions.

###3.2.2 Meta Policy Generator
The Meta Policy Generator translates the complexity indices into a concrete augmentation policy through a three-step procedure. First, distribution diagnostics were performed on the per-feature morphology and context distributions measured by the profiling stage — detecting the modality (valley structure) of each feature — rather than on the scalar indices themselves, so that the candidate space is shaped by the observed distribution shape of each dataset. Second, a candidate policy pool was assembled per axis: for the morphology axis, Gaussian-mixture and Otsu-threshold partitioning; for the context axis, Gaussian-mixture and percentile partitioning. Third, a candidate pruning step was applied to the morphology axis: its candidate pool is shaped by the measured per-feature valley counts and then gated by MCI thresholds (e.g., a hierarchical candidate is admitted only at high MCI and dropped at low MCI), eliminating strategies poorly matched to the observed complexity regime. The context axis retains a fixed two-member pool (Gaussian-mixture and percentile) independent of the complexity indices, so pruning by complexity applies to the morphology axis rather than to both. The surviving candidates were subjected to empirical policy evaluation (by clustering separability) on a small representative subset, and the best-performing policy was selected automatically. Crucially, the policy decision boundary was determined entirely by dataset statistics rather than by domain knowledge, which constitutes the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets. The outcome of this selection procedure on the evaluated datasets is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:policy_selection}, which reports the empirical evaluation scores of the surviving candidate policies and the policy ultimately chosen for each dataset. This makes the otherwise opaque policy decision auditable: on the evaluated roster the morphology axis selected the Otsu policy on all four datasets, whereas the context axis selected Gaussian-mixture partitioning on some datasets and percentile partitioning on others (Table 1), with the choice driven entirely by the measured complexity statistics rather than fixed by hand.

###3.2.3 Stage 1: ROI Extraction
Regions of interest were extracted from the normal images to identify plausible defect placement locations. Otsu thresholding was first applied to each normal image to produce a global foreground mask, and connected-component analysis was then performed to derive candidate bounding boxes, with components smaller than a minimum area of 100 pixels discarded as noise. For each retained region, a five-stage background analysis pipeline classified the surrounding texture into one of five categories: smooth, directional, periodic, organic, or complex. In addition, a continuity score and a stability score were computed for each ROI to quantify, respectively, the spatial coherence of the region and its robustness across the normal-image population. The extracted regions and their associated descriptors were serialized to roi_metadata.json for downstream use. Downstream, the five-category surface label enters ROI selection only as the `background_type` input to an optional subtype-matching quality proxy (active when `--min_quality > 0`); the core placement scoring instead operates on morphology clusters and a 64-pixel context-cell grid (§3.2.6), so with the default gate off the five-category label is largely orthogonal to the selection score.

###3.2.4 Stage 1b: Seed Defect Classification
Seed defects were extracted from the available defective images to provide morphological templates for synthesis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM) was employed to obtain precise defect masks, with Otsu thresholding retained as a fallback when SAM segmentation was unavailable or unreliable. Each segmented defect was assigned to one of five subtypes—linear_scratch, elongated, compact_blob, irregular, or general—based on threshold criteria applied to its linearity, aspect ratio, and solidity. The resulting subtype assignments and associated geometric descriptors were written to seed_profile.json. These generic subtype labels later supply the `defect_subtype` token consumed by the ControlNet conditioning prompt (§3.2.5); no domain-specific defect names enter the pipeline.

###3.2.5 Stage 2: ControlNet-Based Defect Generation
The primary AROMA synthesis engine is a fine-tuned ControlNet generator. For each dataset the ControlNet branch is fine-tuned as the sole trainable module: the Stable Diffusion v1.5 backbone — UNet, VAE, and CLIP text encoder — is frozen and only the ControlNet is optimized. The ControlNet is initialized from the public `lllyasviel/sd-controlnet-canny` checkpoint (its default init weights), but the conditioning is not a raw Canny edge map: the hint image is a custom three-channel structural map produced by `utils/hint_generator.py` — a defect-shape channel (a morphology-dependent skeleton, filled mask, or Canny-of-mask), a Sobel background-edge channel, and a local-variance texture channel — that is collapsed to grayscale and replicated across the three channels so the model cannot exploit a color shortcut. The same hint generator and conditioning text prompt are applied at both training and inference time. The training objective is the standard diffusion noise-prediction loss — an epsilon-prediction MSE between the ControlNet-conditioned UNet output and the added noise — with Min-SNR-gamma loss weighting (gamma = 5.0). Optimization uses AdamW (learning rate 1e-5, weight decay 1e-2) with a cosine schedule and gradient-norm clipping at 1.0, at 512-pixel resolution; the ControlNet weights are kept in fp32 even under mixed-precision training, and a NaN/Inf guard skips affected batches, halves the learning rate on repeated instability, and aborts after a fixed tolerance, so fp16 training does not corrupt the ControlNet gradients. This conditioning prompt is the deterministic `technical`-style prompt of `utils/prompt_generator.py` (`--style technical`), a function of the defect's geometry (linearity, solidity, aspect ratio) together with the target background type and a stability score; it is instantiated with identical inputs in `build_train_jsonl.py` (training) and `generate_defects.py` (inference), and a single fixed negative prompt is shared likewise. This is distinct from the pipeline's separate Step-2 semantic prompt (`prompt_generation.py` → `prompts.json`), a fixed-template `{morphology}, {context}, {prior}` string that is carried in the ROI-candidate metadata but is deliberately discarded at inference as out-of-distribution for the trained model. Parity therefore comes from reusing one generator with identical inputs at both stages — not from the candidate `prompt` field — so training and generation share exactly one conditioning distribution. Training is dataset-adaptive rather than fixed: the number of epochs and the augmentation/color regime are set per dataset from its complexity and surface type (steel Severstal 100 epochs without augmentation and grayscale-forced target; magnetic-tile MTD and tiled AITeX 150 epochs with augmentation and grayscale; color-organic MVTec Leather 300 epochs with augmentation and grayscale disabled), under mixed-precision (fp16) training with gradient checkpointing, while the ControlNet branch is pinned to fp32 for numerical stability. Here "grayscale-forced target" means the target defect crop is converted to a single luminance channel and re-expanded to three identical RGB channels so the SD1.5 VAE latent stays color-neutral (the hint is always grayscale-collapsed regardless of this toggle), and the per-dataset "augmentation" toggle refers to ControlNet training-time image augmentation only — a 50% horizontal flip applied to both target and hint, plus brightness/contrast jitter in [0.9, 1.1] applied to the target alone — which is distinct from the synthetic-data augmentation budget of §3.3.4. Model selection is by lowest per-epoch training loss (there is no held-out validation-loss criterion), with early stopping after a fixed number of non-improving epochs (patience). `[committed: colab_execute_new/step5_execute.md STEP 5b — per-dataset epochs/augment/grayscale/patience.]` At inference the trained model synthesizes a defect appearance conditioned on the selected ROI's structure and prompt, sampling with a UniPC multistep scheduler for 30 denoising steps at ControlNet conditioning scale 0.7 and 512-pixel resolution (the classifier-free guidance scale is left at the diffusers default, not overridden in code), so that — unlike patch reuse — the engine can render (morphology, context) combinations that have few or no real source crops, which is the property that makes deficit- and context-aware selection actionable (§3.2.6, §4.4).

As an aspect-ratio-safe fallback, when the ControlNet generation would distort a highly elongated defect beyond a bounding-box aspect-ratio threshold — the longer bbox side divided by the shorter, default `--cn_ar_threshold` 2.5 (notably for AITeX textile cracks) — the affected ROI is instead composited by direct copy-paste of the real defect crop (recorded as method `copy_paste_arfallback`). This preserves the count, class, and bounding-box parity of the affected samples so the downstream comparison stays balanced, and the fraction routed to it is reported honestly per dataset (a high fallback rate is flagged as "limited ControlNet contribution" rather than hidden). Separately, AROMA also provides a training-free elastic-warping generator (TF-IDG) that produces seed-defect variants by subtype-specific displacement (OpenCV `cv2.remap()`) with brightness/contrast jitter in [0.85, 1.15]; its per-subtype strategies are summarized in Table 2. This is the copy-paste-lineage variant generator and is distinct from both the primary ControlNet engine and the copy-paste AR fallback above.

###3.2.6 Stage 3: Compatibility-Aware Placement (and the Limits of Deficit under Copy-Paste)
Placement of synthetic defects is scored over ROI candidates using a data-driven compatibility model rather than a fixed rule. ROI selection pairs a two-phase coverage-first allocator (`--sampling_strategy deficit_aware`) with a realism score (`--score_mode realism`, top-k=200). The per-candidate score is `0.5·ctx_prior + 0.3·morph_prior + 0.2·quality_score`, in which the deficit term carries weight zero — it was empirically refuted under copy-paste (see below) — so deficit does not enter the score and survives only as a cross-pair quota signal (mean per-pair deficit distributed by largest-remainder) inside the allocator. Source diversity is capped at one instance per distinct source crop (`--img_diversity_cap 1`); however, when a dataset's pool of distinct source crops is smaller than top-k the cap is relaxed one unit at a time until top-k is met, so the one-instance property holds only where the candidate pool is at least top-k (elsewhere the same crops are repeated). For the multi-class datasets a per-class floor of `top_k // K` slots is guaranteed to each of the K classes as an anti-starvation floor; only the `top_k % K` leftover slots are distributed in proportion to per-class deficit mass, so when K divides top-k the cross-class split is uniform rather than deficit-proportional. The committed multi-class run additionally applies a per-pair monoculture cap (`--per_pair_cap_frac 0.05`). Selected ROIs are therefore those the profiling statistics judge physically plausible (dominated by contextual compatibility), spread across `(cluster, context)` pairs by the deficit-weighted quota rather than by any deficit term in the score. At composition time, a symmetric compatibility gate decides where each generated defect may be placed: the profiling stage emits a symmetric defect–background compatibility model (`matrix_symmetric`, together with a per-patch defect distribution `P_def_patch`, a clean-background distribution, and an additive Laplace smoothing constant ε used inside the geometric-mean compatibility `compat = sqrt((P_def+ε)·(P_clean+ε))` — a smoothing constant, not an acceptance-tolerance band, since acceptance is governed by τ), and each candidate placement is scored by matching the defect against a 64-pixel tiled query of the target region and accepted only if its compatibility exceeds a dataset-specific threshold τ. τ is fixed by a CPU pre-scan to a target rejection rate before any downstream number is seen (τ ≠ 0.5), never tuned post hoc. A clean-background gate additionally rejects placements onto degenerate flat/blank regions. This "positive placement" (scan → rank → place) replaces uniform random placement in the AROMA arm; the random arm keeps the identical clean-background gate but no compatibility ranking, so the two arms differ only in placement policy. The symmetric compatibility model itself is counted from where defect and clean-background contexts co-occur across the dataset's own patches rather than hand-authored, making `matrix_symmetric` the automatically re-derived, per-dataset analogue of CASDA's handcrafted compatibility matrix. `[committed: colab_execute_new/step3_execute.md, step4_execute.md, step5_execute.md §τ pre-scan.]`

A mechanistic finding qualifies what this placement machinery can and cannot do. The deficit-aware component, taken alone, is **structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis**: the (morphology, context) combinations with the highest deficit are, by construction, those rarely observed in the real data—and therefore those for which no source crop exists to copy. Deficit is thus orthogonal to source availability—high-deficit bins are unfillable, fillable bins have low deficit. In the profiled datasets the highest-deficit bins contained no candidate crops `[committed: deficit_actionability_diagnosis — leather 0 actionable of 134 non-zero deficit bins; mtd 40 of 430, max ≈0.051]`, so under copy-paste the deficit term made no difference and the sole copy-paste positive (tiled AITeX, Table 12) occurred with zero deficit among its selected ROIs, driven instead by contextual compatibility and per-candidate realism `[committed: aitex_positive_reverification — selected deficit>0: aitex 0/200; ctx_prior +0.427, quality_score +0.30 candidate→selected median]`.

This is precisely the limitation the ControlNet engine removes. Because the generator can *synthesize* a defect for a targeted (morphology, context) bin rather than requiring a real crop to copy, compatibility-aware placement becomes actionable under generation—and the downstream evidence bears this out: the same selection policy that was inert under copy-paste on Severstal produces AROMA's largest, all-seed-consistent gain under the ControlNet engine (+0.096 mAP@0.5; §4.4, Table 13). Defect composites are then formed by seamless blending onto the assigned background (Stage 4).

###3.2.7 Stage 4: Seamless Blending Synthesis
Each generated (or, in the fallback path, copy-pasted) defect was composited onto its assigned normal background with seamless (gradient-domain) cloning via OpenCV `seamlessClone` in `NORMAL_CLONE` mode, centered on the placement footprint and preceded by a Reinhard Lab-space color/illumination transfer from the local background patch (luminance-only for grayscale surfaces such as steel). Gradient-domain cloning reconciles the gradients of the defect and the background and thereby strongly suppresses the boundary discontinuities characteristic of naive copy-paste compositing; residual artifacts can remain, which is precisely why the quality gate of §3.2.8 measures a boundary-artifact score. When seamless cloning is not applicable — OpenCV unavailable, a mask smaller than 16 pixels, an out-of-bounds footprint, or an OpenCV failure — the pipeline falls back to Gaussian-feathered alpha compositing; the placement decision is unchanged, only the compositing operator differs. The same blend mode (`--blend_mode seamless`) is applied identically to the AROMA and random arms, so blending is not a confounding factor between them. For every synthesized image, the corresponding defect mask was co-saved alongside the composite, providing pixel-level localization; the co-saved bounding box is the pasted crop's footprint in composite coordinates (`[x, y, w, h]` — the placement rectangle, not a tight-mask box or the whole frame), which serves as the label for the downstream detector.

###3.2.8 Stage 5: Quality Gate
A quality gate was applied to filter low-fidelity synthetic samples prior to dataset construction. Two metrics were computed for each candidate image: an artifact score, which quantifies spurious high-frequency content introduced during compositing, and a blur score, which measures local sharpness within the defect region. Both metrics were evaluated over a 31×31 dilated ROI mask so that scoring was confined to the defect neighborhood rather than the entire image. A composite quality score Q was derived from these metrics, and samples falling below the quality threshold were discarded.

##3.3. Experimental Procedure

###3.3.1 Dataset Split
Train–test partitioning followed the on-disk structure provided by each source dataset (Severstal, MVTec Leather, AITeX, MTD); where a dataset lacked a predefined split, a fixed-seed partition was applied for reproducibility. In all cases, synthetic images were added exclusively to the training partition, and the test partition consisted solely of real images, enforcing strict isolation between synthetic data and evaluation. All downstream results are reported over three seeds (1, 2, 42), with paired testing across the baseline / random / AROMA conditions on a shared real test set.

###3.3.2 Downstream Detection Protocol
Downstream utility was assessed with supervised defect detection: a YOLOv8n detector (COCO-pretrained, trained fresh under each condition) was trained under each of the three data conditions (baseline / random / AROMA) and evaluated by mean average precision at IoU 0.5 (mAP@0.5) on the shared real test set. The multi-class datasets (Severstal, MVTec Leather, MTD) use `--class_mode multi` with the class set enumerated per dataset, imgsz 640, and rectangular training; tiled AITeX uses a single defect class at imgsz 256 without rectangular training (tiles are square). Training runs to a fixed budget with early stopping (baseline_epochs 100 for the multi-class datasets, 300 for tiled AITeX; patience 50; validation fraction 0.3; batch 64). This supervised-detection protocol, rather than the one-class AD architectures explored in earlier drafts, is the committed evaluation and the source of all downstream numbers reported in §4.4. `[committed: colab_execute_new/_SPEC.md §4, exp4v2_execute.md.]` `[NOTE — the previously drafted four one-class AD architectures (PatchCore, Supersimplenet, EfficientAD, ReverseDistillation++) and their §4.3 AUROC table were not run under the committed protocol and have been withdrawn; see §4.3.]`

###3.3.3 ROI Coverage Evaluation
The capacity of the ROI selection policy to span the space of defect placements was assessed through a coverage analysis comparing the AROMA policy against random selection. Five metrics were reported: morphology_coverage and context_coverage, which measure the proportion of distinct morphology and context categories represented; rare_pair_coverage, which quantifies representation of infrequent (morphology, context) combinations; entropy, which measures the uniformity of the selection's morphology (cluster_id) frequency distribution; and the Gini coefficient, which measures the inequality of that same morphology distribution. Higher coverage and entropy and lower Gini values indicate a more balanced and comprehensive exploration of the placement space. These are neutral descriptors of the selection distribution; they do not by themselves establish a coverage advantage for AROMA. On the committed selection maps the anticipated rare-pair/coverage advantage was in fact not observed — the deficit term that was intended to drive rare-pair coverage is inert under copy-paste (§3.2.6) — and the corresponding statistics (Table 8) remain frozen pending committed result files (§4.1).

###3.3.4 Augmentation Budget
For each dataset, both augmented conditions (Random and AROMA) add synthetic defect images to the real training partition at an identical synth:real budget (synth_ratio = 1.0, i.e. one synthetic image per real training image, capped per dataset to enforce n_synth parity across arms), so that the two conditions differ only in the ROI-selection strategy and not in the amount of synthetic data. Both arms pass through the same clean-background gate (rejecting degenerate flat/blank placements): a placement is accepted only if a composite background-quality score — a weighted combination of blur (Laplacian variance), local contrast, brightness, and noise — meets a minimum of 0.7, with the blur (sharpness) threshold set to 100, and the fraction of AITeX samples routed to the aspect-ratio-safe TF-IDG fallback (§3.2.5) is recorded and reported so that the ControlNet contribution is not overstated. `[committed: colab_execute_new/step4_execute.md — synth_ratio 1.0, clean-bg gate parameters, AR-fallback reporting. The per-dataset quality-gate pass rate is a per-run diagnostic not fixed in the committed configuration and is not asserted here.]`

#4. Experimental Results and Analysis
This section reports the empirical evaluation of AROMA across the industrial datasets of §3.1 (Severstal, MVTec Leather, AITeX, MTD) along three complementary axes. Experiment 0 (Section 4.1) evaluates the quality of the ROI selection policy through coverage and distributional statistics, isolating the contribution of the data-driven placement mechanism. Experiment 1 (Section 4.2) assesses the perceptual quality of the synthesized images. Experiment 2 (Section 4.4) measures the downstream impact of the augmented data on supervised detection performance (mAP@0.5), which constitutes the primary empirical evidence and is reported honestly and conditionally. Throughout, AROMA is compared against a random ROI placement baseline (Random) and a real-only baseline (Baseline) to disentangle the effect of context-aware placement from that of synthetic data volume alone.

##4.4. Supervised Detection Evaluation (Exp4v2)

The downstream evaluation in Section 4.3 follows the one-class anomaly detection (AD) protocol, in which only normal images are used for training and synthetic defects augment the nominal training distribution. During post-hoc auditing, we identified a protocol concern with that one-class setup: in the exp4 configuration, defect-bearing synthetic images were inadvertently routed into the nominal ("good") training partition, which violates the one-class assumption that the training set contains only defect-free samples and confounds the interpretation of the AUROC gains. To obtain a measurement free of this confound, we designed a separate supervised detection experiment (Exp4v2) in which defects are treated as explicitly labeled foreground objects and the synthetic data is used as a conventional labeled training set rather than as a perturbation of a nominal manifold.

Exp4v2 employs YOLOv8 detectors under a baseline-then-augment protocol: a model is first trained on the real training partition (Baseline), and each augmented condition adds synthetic defect images—with their co-saved ROI masks converted to bounding-box labels—to the same training partition at a fixed synth:real budget. Ground-truth masks of the held-out real test split are converted to YOLO-format boxes for evaluation; detection quality is reported as mAP@0.5 over 3 seeds (seeds 1, 2, 42) with paired testing. #5. Discussion
The principal contribution of AROMA is methodological: a Meta Policy Generator that selects ROI modeling and placement policies automatically from measurable dataset complexity statistics (MCI, CCI), removing the per-domain handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules that its predecessor CASDA required. This design advance holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces (metal, organic, textile, machined ceramic) without per-dataset re-engineering. The Meta Policy Generator's Distribution Diagnostics and Candidate Pruning components are described as a design mechanism for making the policy decision reproducible from statistics; we do not attach validated ablation figures (e.g., an MCI/CCI weighting-robustness correlation) to these components in this manuscript, as no such ablation is backed by a committed run, and any such claim is deferred until measured.

Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally, and the headline is engine-dependent. Under the training-free copy-paste ablation (Table 12), AROMA and random ROI placement are statistically indistinguishable in the dominant case — the difference lies within seed-to-seed variation on every dataset except tiled AITeX — because both arms composite the same defect pixels and differ only in placement context. Under the primary ControlNet + seamless engine (Table 13), the picture changes on the hardest dataset: **AROMA exceeds random by +0.096 mAP@0.5 on Severstal, consistently across all three seeds**, which is null under copy-paste. The two remaining multi-class datasets (MTD ≈0.92, MVTec Leather ≈0.83–0.91) are near-ceiling and saturate under random augmentation regardless of engine, and AITeX is a small positive within seed noise; the benefit is therefore conditional on both baseline headroom and a generative engine, not uniform. Full-image distribution metrics (FID, KID, LPIPS) are reported only qualitatively (Tables 9–10 frozen); under copy-paste they are near-identical between arms by construction, and even under generation they measure the global distribution rather than the localized placement effect that supervised detection (§4.4) captures, which we therefore treat as the primary evidence.

We also contribute a mechanistic negative result that explains this pattern at the signal level. The deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting that motivated much of this line of work is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis: the highest-deficit (morphology, context) combinations are precisely those with no real source patch to copy, so deficit is orthogonal to source availability (§3.2.6). Consistent with this, under copy-paste the single AITeX positive occurred with zero deficit among its selected ROIs and was driven by contextual compatibility and per-candidate realism, not deficit. The intended value of deficit-aware targeting — filling rare combinations with sources that do not otherwise exist — can only be realized by coupling data-driven placement with a generative engine, and the Severstal result under the ControlNet engine (+0.096 over random, all seeds) is the confirmation: the identical selection policy that was inert under patch reuse becomes effective once the engine can synthesize the targeted combinations. This corroborates, rather than merely conjectures, the generative-engine claim. (The elongated aspect ratios of AITeX defects remain a known difficulty for the generator, handled by the TF-IDG fallback and reported honestly.)

**Limitations.** Several limitations bound the interpretation of these results and are stated explicitly to forestall over-generalization. First, the headline positive (Severstal, +0.096 mAP@0.5 over random, all 3 seeds under the ControlNet engine) is a single-dataset result measured at one detector size (YOLOv8n); replication across detector sizes and the CASDA-ROI comparison (Table 14) are still pending, so it should be read as a strong conditional positive, not a general improvement. Second, the deficit-aware mechanism does not drive gains under copy-paste and is refuted as a source of benefit for patch reuse; its value is realized only through the generative engine. Third, near-ceiling datasets (MTD, MVTec Leather) saturate under random augmentation regardless of engine, so the benefit is confined to datasets with substantial baseline headroom. Fourth, the gain is confounded between selection strategy and candidate pool; a symmetric-filter control (applying the same compatibility/quality top-filter to the random arm) is required before attributing it wholly to selection rather than pool composition. Fifth, placement is only physically feasible on a subset of datasets: measured clean-background coverage of in-matrix context cells is highly uneven (leather 4.7%, MTD 67.2%, AITeX 77.8%), so context-matched placement is realizable primarily on AITeX, and for full-frame datasets such as Severstal clean-background gating is not meaningful. Finally, the copy-paste and ControlNet four-dataset results are committed over 3 seeds (mean ± standard deviation); the four-condition Severstal comparison that adds the CASDA-ROI arm across three detector sizes (Table 14) is not yet committed.

#6. Conclusion
We presented AROMA, a framework that automatically selects ROI modeling and placement policies from dataset complexity statistics (MCI and CCI) via a Meta Policy Generator, removing the per-domain handcrafted re-engineering required by prior context-aware synthesis such as CASDA. The principal contribution is this methodological framework for automatic, per-dataset policy selection, which holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces without per-dataset re-engineering. Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally rather than as a uniform performance improvement. Under a training-free copy-paste engine, AROMA and random ROI placement are statistically indistinguishable in the dominant case, and we explain this with a mechanistic result: deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting is structurally inert under patch reuse because high-deficit combinations lack any real source patch to copy (deficit ⟂ source availability). Under the primary ControlNet + seamless generative engine, the same context-aware selection policy produces its largest and only all-seed-consistent gain on the hardest, highest-headroom dataset — Severstal, +0.096 mAP@0.5 over random, exactly where it was null under copy-paste — while near-ceiling datasets converge with random augmentation. This confirms, rather than merely conjectures, that context/deficit-aware ROI selection becomes effective only when coupled with an engine that can synthesize the targeted combinations. This work therefore both delimits when ROI selection fails (patch reuse, near-ceiling regimes) and demonstrates when it helps (a generative engine with baseline headroom). Future work should replicate the Severstal gain across detector sizes, run the symmetric-filter control to separate selection from pool effects, and complete the four-condition CASDA-ROI comparison (Table 14).
