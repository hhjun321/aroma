#Abstract
Anomaly detection in industrial Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) is constrained by severe data sparsity and class imbalance, as defect samples are rare and morphologically heterogeneous in real production environments. Prior context-aware synthesis frameworks, such as CASDA, mitigate this scarcity by encoding defect–background relationships, but rely on domain-specific handcrafted rules and a manually constructed compatibility matrix that must be re-engineered for each new dataset, limiting their scalability. We propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), an eight-stage framework whose primary contribution is methodological: it automatically selects ROI modeling policies from dataset complexity statistics, eliminating the per-dataset re-engineering required by handcrafted approaches. AROMA computes a Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and a Context Complexity Index (CCI), which a Meta Policy Generator translates into clustering and placement policies through distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation. Alongside this framework, we report two honest empirical findings that qualify when synthetic ROI selection helps. First, in multi-domain downstream detection, AROMA is largely statistically indistinguishable from random ROI placement; a measurable benefit appears only on a single dataset that combines valid measurement, ample baseline headroom, and physically feasible context-matched placement, while datasets whose detectors are already near-ceiling converge with random augmentation. Second, we show that the deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting motivating much of this line of work is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis, because the combinations with the highest deficit are precisely those for which no source patch exists to copy (deficit is orthogonal to source availability). We frame this negative result as a mechanistic contribution explaining why rare-combination targeting cannot operate without a generative engine. #1. Introduction
With the progression of smart manufacturing, defect inspection in industrial production has shifted from manual examination to deep learning-based Automated Visual Inspection (AVI) systems [1]. These systems now achieve high reliability across diverse sectors, including steel, semiconductors, and printed circuit boards, and have established themselves as a core technology for quality assurance. In particular, one-class anomaly detection models that learn solely from normal data have attracted considerable attention, as they avoid the prohibitive cost of collecting and annotating exhaustive defect samples [2]. Nevertheless, the performance of these models still depends heavily on the diversity and representativeness of the available training data, which limits their effectiveness in data-scarce industrial settings [3].

In real-world manufacturing environments, acquiring sufficient defect samples is inherently difficult because defects occur infrequently and exhibit highly variable shapes, scales, and textures [4]. This scarcity of minority-class defects produces severe class imbalance, which degrades the generalization capability of detection models [5]. Conventional augmentation techniques, such as geometric transformations and color perturbations, partially expand data diversity but fail to capture the structural properties of defects or their contextual relationship with the surrounding background [6]. More recently, generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and diffusion-based methods, have shown promise in synthesizing realistic defects [7, 8]; however, they require substantial computational resources and frequently produce samples that are physically inconsistent with the target substrate.

To address these limitations, context-aware synthesis has emerged as an effective strategy that explicitly models the relationship between defect morphology and background surface properties. CASDA (Context-Aware Steel Defect Augmentation) exemplifies this paradigm by constructing a compatibility matrix that encodes defect–background coexistence probabilities and synthesizing contextually consistent samples through a conditional generative pipeline [9]. Such context-aware approaches have demonstrated that respecting the structural relationship between a defect and its substrate yields more discriminative training samples than context-agnostic patch reuse.

Despite this progress, existing context-aware frameworks share a critical scalability bottleneck: their conditioning mechanisms are designed manually for a specific domain. CASDA, for instance, relies on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific morphological rules tuned for steel surfaces, and applying it to a new dataset requires substantial re-engineering of these components. Consequently, the question of how ROI modeling and placement policies can be derived automatically from intrinsic dataset characteristics—rather than crafted by hand for each domain—remains insufficiently explored. A data-driven mechanism that adapts the augmentation policy to the complexity of an arbitrary industrial dataset would substantially improve the generality of context-aware synthesis.

To this end, we propose AROMA (Adaptive ROI-based Morphology-Aware Augmentation), a framework that overcomes the per-domain engineering burden by learning ROI modeling policies directly from dataset complexity statistics. AROMA first characterizes each dataset through two quantitative descriptors—the Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) and the Context Complexity Index (CCI)—and a Meta Policy Generator translates these statistics into appropriate clustering and placement policies via distribution diagnostics, candidate pruning, and empirical evaluation on a small subset. Guided by these policies, the pipeline extracts and classifies ROIs, applies subtype-specific training-free elastic warping, optimizes defect placement through deficit-aware sampling, and composites defects onto normal backgrounds using Poisson blending. The principal contributions of this work are summarized as follows:

- **A data-driven complexity policy framework.** We introduce MCI and CCI statistics that, through a Meta Policy Generator, automatically determine ROI modeling and placement policies, eliminating the per-dataset handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules required by prior context-aware frameworks such as CASDA. This contribution is a design/scalability advance and is independent of downstream accuracy: the same pipeline is applied across heterogeneous datasets without per-domain re-engineering.
- **An auditable policy-selection mechanism.** We design a Meta Policy Generator that selects among candidate clustering and context-partitioning policies through distribution diagnostics, complexity-ranked candidate pruning, and empirical policy evaluation, making the otherwise opaque policy decision reproducible from measured statistics.
- **A mechanistic negative result on deficit-aware copy-paste.** We empirically demonstrate that deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis: the highest-deficit (morphology, context) combinations are those with no real source crop to copy, so deficit is orthogonal to source availability. We show that the single dataset on which AROMA outperformed random placement did so with zero deficit in its selected ROIs, whereas the dataset with the highest selected deficit showed no benefit. This clarifies why rare-combination targeting requires a generative engine rather than patch reuse.
- **An honest, conditional downstream evaluation.** We evaluate AROMA across multiple industrial datasets and report, without cherry-picking, that AROMA and random ROI placement are statistically indistinguishable in the dominant case; a downstream benefit is observed only under the conjunction of valid measurement, baseline headroom, and feasible context-matched placement (a single dataset, n=1), and we analyze the confounds and ceiling effects that bound this result.

#2. Related Work
##2.1. Industrial Defect Detection
Automated industrial defect detection has traditionally been formulated as a supervised learning problem, in which object detection and segmentation networks are trained on densely annotated defect images to localize surface anomalies [1,2]. While such fully supervised approaches achieve strong performance when abundant labeled defects are available, their reliance on large annotated datasets is impractical in real manufacturing settings, where defects are rare and labeling is costly [3]. To address this constraint, one-class anomaly detection methods have emerged as the dominant paradigm, learning a model of normality exclusively from defect-free images and flagging deviations at inference time. Representative methods such as PatchCore [4], EfficientAD [5], SimpleNet [6], and Reverse Distillation [7] achieve high detection accuracy by modeling the feature distribution of normal samples, yet their performance degrades when the available normal data is limited or when subtle, structurally diverse defects must be distinguished from benign variation [8]. Consequently, augmenting the limited pool of defect data has become a central concern for robust industrial anomaly detection.

##2.2. Data Augmentation for Defect Images
Conventional data augmentation expands training sets through geometric transformations (rotation, flipping, scaling) and photometric perturbations (brightness, contrast, color jitter) [9]. Although computationally inexpensive and widely adopted, these label-preserving operations only resample the existing appearance of defects and fail to introduce the structural variability that characterizes real industrial anomalies [10]. Cut-paste strategies partially address this gap by extracting defect patches and inserting them into normal images to synthesize new defective samples [11,12]. However, naive cut-paste frequently produces visually implausible composites, since achieving natural alignment between the pasted defect and the surrounding background texture, illumination, and geometry remains a challenging and largely unsolved problem [13]. As a result, augmentation realism—rather than mere quantity—has become the key bottleneck for downstream detection gains.

##2.3. Generative Models for Industrial Augmentation
Generative models have been increasingly explored as a means of synthesizing diverse and realistic defect imagery. Generative adversarial networks (GANs) have been applied to defect synthesis [14,15], but they are prone to mode collapse and training instability, which limit the diversity of the generated defects and demand substantial per-dataset tuning. Diffusion-based approaches, particularly denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs), produce markedly higher sample diversity and fidelity [16,17], establishing themselves as the current state of the art for image synthesis. Nevertheless, most generative pipelines model the defect appearance in isolation and do not explicitly account for the background context in which a defect must plausibly appear [18]. This common gap—generating defects without principled background modeling—motivates synthesis methods that jointly reason about defect and context.

##2.4. Context-Aware Defect Synthesis
A growing line of work seeks to incorporate spatial and contextual constraints into the defect generation process. ControlNet-based conditioning, for example, has been used to guide diffusion models with masks or layout cues so that synthesized defects respect the structure of the host image [19,20]. Other methods explicitly model defect–background compatibility, selecting or generating defects that are consistent with the local texture and semantics of the target region [21]. CASDA [22] is a representative example of this direction, employing a handcrafted compatibility matrix to match defect types with appropriate background regions during synthesis. While effective on its target domain, CASDA's reliance on manually engineered, domain-specific rules limits its transferability, requiring substantial re-engineering for each new dataset.

##2.5. Copy-Paste and ROI-based Synthesis
Copy-paste augmentation, popularized by Dwibedi et al. [11] and later refined for instance segmentation [12], remains an attractive baseline because it preserves authentic defect pixels while avoiding the training cost and artifacts of generative models. The effectiveness of such methods, however, depends heavily on where defects are placed: the selection of regions of interest (ROIs) for placement strongly influences the realism and the downstream utility of the synthesized data [23]. Most existing approaches adopt random or uniform ROI selection, which is domain-agnostic but suboptimal, as it ignores the morphological and contextual compatibility between a defect and its candidate location [24]. To date, principled, data-driven learning of ROI placement policies has received little attention, leaving the choice of placement strategy largely heuristic.

##2.6. Data-Driven Pipeline Adaptation
Beyond individual augmentation operators, recent research has investigated automatically adapting pipeline configurations to the characteristics of the data. Meta-learning and AutoML techniques optimize augmentation policies and architectural choices directly from data rather than relying on manual design [25,26], while complexity-guided feature learning leverages quantitative descriptors of dataset difficulty to steer model behavior [27]. These efforts demonstrate that measurable dataset statistics can serve as effective signals for configuring learning pipelines without hand-crafted rules. Building on this insight, AROMA introduces a novel direction by applying dataset complexity statistics—captured through morphology and context complexity indices—to drive the automatic selection of ROI modeling and placement policies. In contrast to CASDA's handcrafted, single-domain compatibility matrix, this data-driven formulation enables the synthesis pipeline to generalize across heterogeneous industrial datasets without per-dataset re-engineering.

#3. Methodology

For each dataset, three experimental conditions were compared under an identical evaluation protocol. The baseline condition employed only real normal images for training, representing the standard unsupervised anomaly detection setting. The random-ROI condition augmented the training set with synthetic defect images in which defect placement regions were sampled uniformly at random. The AROMA-ROI condition augmented the training set with synthetic defect images generated under the proposed data-driven policy, in which placement regions and seed defects were selected according to learned complexity policies. All three conditions shared the same real test set, and synthetic imagery was confined exclusively to the training partition to preserve strict train–test isolation.

The AROMA pipeline is organized as a sequence of stages that progress from dataset-level complexity quantification through automated policy selection to defect synthesis and quality control. In contrast to its predecessor CASDA, which relied on a handcrafted compatibility matrix and domain-specific rules tuned for a single dataset, AROMA derives its augmentation policy directly from measurable dataset statistics. ###3.2.1 Phase 0: Dataset Complexity Analysis
The pipeline begins by quantifying the intrinsic complexity of each dataset along two orthogonal axes: defect morphology and background context. Two scalar indices were computed for this purpose. The Morphology Complexity Index (MCI) characterizes the structural variability of defect-bearing regions and was defined as the mean of four z-score-normalized components:

MCI = Mean(Entropy, ValleyCount, ClusterCount, 1 − Silhouette).

Here, Entropy captures intensity-distribution disorder, ValleyCount reflects the multimodality of the intensity histogram, ClusterCount denotes the number of distinct morphological groupings, and the Silhouette term measures cluster separability, with its complement (1 − Silhouette) increasing as morphological groupings become less separable. The Context Complexity Index (CCI) characterizes background heterogeneity and was defined as the mean of four normalized components:

CCI = Mean(TextureEntropy, ClusterDiversity, FreqComplexity, OrientVariance),

where TextureEntropy quantifies local texture disorder, ClusterDiversity measures the variety of background regions, FreqComplexity captures spectral richness in the frequency domain, and OrientVariance reflects the dispersion of dominant gradient orientations. To ensure equitable comparison across datasets of differing scale, the per-class valley count was computed as a class-wise mean, and contextual statistics were estimated from a sub-sample of 20,000 image patches per dataset. The resulting MCI and CCI values were used to drive subsequent policy selection, providing a quantitative substitute for the domain expertise previously required by CASDA. Figure~\ref{fig:complexity_landscape} positions the four evaluated datasets in the MCI–CCI complexity plane, confirming that the selected datasets span a broad and well-separated range of morphological and contextual difficulty rather than clustering in a single regime; this spread is what enables the policy-selection mechanism to be exercised across distinct complexity conditions.

###3.2.2 Meta Policy Generator
The Meta Policy Generator translates the complexity indices into a concrete augmentation policy through a three-step procedure. First, distribution diagnostics were performed on the MCI and CCI scores to characterize their statistical structure. Second, a candidate policy pool was assembled, comprising Gaussian Mixture Model, K-Means, and Hierarchical clustering strategies for morphology modeling, together with grid-based and patch-based strategies for context modeling. Third, a candidate pruning step was applied in which the complexity rank derived from MCI and CCI reduced the pool to at most two or three candidates, eliminating strategies poorly matched to the observed complexity regime. The surviving candidates were subjected to empirical policy evaluation on a small representative subset, and the best-performing policy was selected automatically. Crucially, the policy decision boundary was determined entirely by dataset statistics rather than by domain knowledge, which constitutes the central mechanism by which AROMA generalizes across datasets. The outcome of this selection procedure on the evaluated datasets is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:policy_selection}, which reports the empirical evaluation scores of the surviving candidate policies and the algorithm ultimately chosen for each dataset. This makes the otherwise opaque policy decision auditable: rather than fixing a single clustering or placement rule by hand, AROMA selects, for example, Gaussian Mixture clustering for datasets with overlapping continuous morphology and K-Means for datasets with compact, well-separated clusters, with the choice driven entirely by the measured complexity statistics.

###3.2.3 Stage 1: ROI Extraction
Regions of interest were extracted from the normal images to identify plausible defect placement locations. Otsu thresholding was first applied to each normal image to produce a global foreground mask, and connected-component analysis was then performed to derive candidate bounding boxes, with components smaller than a minimum area of 100 pixels discarded as noise. For each retained region, a five-stage background analysis pipeline classified the surrounding texture into one of five categories: smooth, directional, periodic, organic, or complex. In addition, a continuity score and a stability score were computed for each ROI to quantify, respectively, the spatial coherence of the region and its robustness across the normal-image population. The extracted regions and their associated descriptors were serialized to roi_metadata.json for downstream use.

###3.2.4 Stage 1b: Seed Defect Classification
Seed defects were extracted from the available defective images to provide morphological templates for synthesis. The Segment Anything Model (SAM) was employed to obtain precise defect masks, with Otsu thresholding retained as a fallback when SAM segmentation was unavailable or unreliable. Each segmented defect was assigned to one of five subtypes—linear_scratch, elongated, compact_blob, irregular, or general—based on threshold criteria applied to its linearity, aspect ratio, and solidity. The resulting subtype assignments and associated geometric descriptors were written to seed_profile.json.

###3.2.5 Stage 2: Training-Free Elastic Warping (TF-IDG)
Synthetic defect variants were generated through Training-Free Industrial Defect Generation (TF-IDG), an elastic-warping procedure implemented entirely with OpenCV operations and requiring no deep learning generative model. Each seed defect was deformed using cv2.remap() under a subtype-specific displacement strategy, ensuring that the geometric character of each morphology class was preserved during augmentation. The subtype-specific warping strategies are summarized in Table 2. Following deformation, brightness and contrast jitter were applied within the range [0.85, 1.15] to introduce photometric diversity without altering defect structure.

**Table 2.** Subtype-specific elastic warping strategies.

| Seed subtype | Displacement strategy | Geometric property preserved |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| linear_scratch | Low-amplitude transverse displacement along principal axis | Linearity |
| elongated | Anisotropic warping favoring the major axis | Aspect ratio |
| compact_blob | Isotropic radial displacement | Compactness |
| irregular | High-amplitude multidirectional displacement | Boundary irregularity |
| general | Moderate uniform displacement | Overall shape |

###3.2.6 Stage 3: Placement Optimization and the Limits of Deficit-Aware Targeting
Placement of synthetic defects was scored over the Cartesian product of ROI candidates and seed variants. The pipeline originally incorporated a deficit-aware sampling component intended to prioritize underrepresented (morphology, context) pairs and thereby steer synthesis toward rare combinations. During evaluation, however, we found this component to be **structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis**, and we report this finding rather than presenting deficit-aware sampling as a source of gain.

The reason is a fundamental property of patch reuse: the (morphology, context) combinations with the highest deficit are, by construction, those that are rarely observed in the real data—and therefore those for which no source crop exists to copy. Deficit is thus orthogonal to source availability: high-deficit bins are unfillable, and fillable bins have low deficit. Concretely, in the profiled datasets the highest-deficit bins contained no candidate crops `[committed: deficit_actionability_diagnosis — leather 0 actionable of 134 non-zero deficit bins; mtd 40 of 430, max ≈0.051]`, so the deficit term made no difference to selection. Consistent with this, the only dataset on which AROMA outperformed random placement did so with **zero deficit among its selected ROIs**, whereas the dataset with the largest selected deficit showed no benefit `[committed: aitex_positive_reverification — selected deficit>0: aitex 0/200, mtd 50/200]`. The signals that actually dominated the selection there were contextual compatibility (`ctx_prior`) and per-candidate realism (`quality_score`), not deficit `[committed: aitex_positive_reverification — ctx_prior +0.427, quality_score +0.30 from candidate to selected median]`.

Accordingly, the deficit term contributes no measurable effect under copy-paste, and its intended value—filling rare combinations with sources that do not otherwise exist—could only be realized in combination with a generative engine (e.g., ControlNet), a direction we discuss in §5. Defect composites are formed by Poisson blending onto the assigned background (Stage 4).

###3.2.7 Stage 4: Poisson Blending Synthesis
Each selected seed defect was composited onto its assigned normal background using Poisson blending, which seamlessly reconciles the gradients of the defect and the background and thereby eliminates the boundary discontinuities characteristic of naive copy-paste compositing. For every synthesized image, the corresponding ROI mask was co-saved alongside the composite, providing pixel-level defect localization for downstream training and evaluation.

###3.2.8 Stage 5: Quality Gate
A quality gate was applied to filter low-fidelity synthetic samples prior to dataset construction. Two metrics were computed for each candidate image: an artifact score, which quantifies spurious high-frequency content introduced during compositing, and a blur score, which measures local sharpness within the defect region. Both metrics were evaluated over a 31×31 dilated ROI mask so that scoring was confined to the defect neighborhood rather than the entire image. A composite quality score Q was derived from these metrics, and samples falling below the quality threshold were discarded.

##3.3. Experimental Procedure

###3.3.1 Dataset Split
Train–test partitioning followed the on-disk structure provided by each source dataset (Severstal, MVTec Leather, AITeX, MTD); where a dataset lacked a predefined split, a fixed-seed partition was applied for reproducibility. In all cases, synthetic images were added exclusively to the training partition, and the test partition consisted solely of real images, enforcing strict isolation between synthetic data and evaluation. All downstream results are reported over three seeds (1, 2, 42), with paired testing across the baseline / random / AROMA conditions on a shared real test set.

###3.3.2 Downstream Detection Protocol
Downstream utility was assessed with supervised defect detection: a YOLOv8 detector was trained under each of the three data conditions (baseline / random / AROMA) and evaluated by mean average precision at IoU 0.5 (mAP@0.5) on the shared real test set, in a multi-class configuration where the class set is enumerated per dataset. This supervised-detection protocol, rather than the one-class AD architectures explored in earlier drafts, is the committed evaluation and the source of all downstream numbers reported in §4.4. `[NOTE — the previously drafted four one-class AD architectures (PatchCore, Supersimplenet, EfficientAD, ReverseDistillation++) and their §4.3 AUROC table were not run under the committed protocol and have been withdrawn; see §4.3.]`

###3.3.3 ROI Coverage Evaluation
The capacity of the ROI selection policy to span the space of defect placements was assessed through a coverage analysis comparing the AROMA policy against random selection. Five metrics were reported: morphology_coverage and context_coverage, which measure the proportion of distinct morphology and context categories represented; rare_pair_coverage, which quantifies representation of infrequent (morphology, context) combinations; entropy, which measures the uniformity of the selection distribution; and the Gini coefficient, which measures its inequality. Higher coverage and entropy and lower Gini values indicate a more balanced and comprehensive exploration of the placement space.

###3.3.4 Augmentation Statistics
For each dataset and each augmentation condition, 600 synthetic images were generated to ensure a consistent augmentation budget across experimental settings. The quality-gate pass rate—the fraction of generated candidates retained after the Stage 5 filtering described in Section 3.2.8—was recorded for each dataset to characterize the fidelity of the synthesis process under differing complexity regimes.

We expect only small AROMA-vs-Random differences on any full-image distribution metric, and state the reason qualitatively rather than reporting unbacked numbers. This expectation follows directly from the training-free copy-paste design of the synthesis pipeline: because AROMA and Random both composite the same elastically warped seed defects onto normal backgrounds via Poisson blending, the defect pixels themselves are identical between the two conditions, and only the ROI placement context—the local background texture surrounding each defect—differs. Since FID measures the global image distribution rather than the localized defect region, it is largely insensitive to the placement differences that AROMA is designed to exploit, and the small observed deltas are consistent with this expectation.

This limitation mirrors the FID behavior reported for CASDA, where the copy-paste variant attained an artificially low FID by reusing real patches whose feature distribution coincided with the reference set by construction. The same structural property applies here: full-image distribution metrics cannot fully resolve the contextual placement advantage of AROMA and are therefore reported primarily to characterize, rather than to discriminate, synthesis quality. KID and LPIPS (Table 10) are expected to behave identically for the same reason and are likewise reported only once computed from committed files. Accordingly, the downstream supervised detection performance presented in Section 4.4 is treated as the primary evidence, since it directly reflects the discriminative value of the placement context that distribution-level metrics do not capture.

##4.4. Supervised Detection Evaluation (Exp4v2)

The downstream evaluation in Section 4.3 follows the one-class anomaly detection (AD) protocol, in which only normal images are used for training and synthetic defects augment the nominal training distribution. During post-hoc auditing, we identified a protocol concern with that one-class setup: in the exp4 configuration, defect-bearing synthetic images were inadvertently routed into the nominal ("good") training partition, which violates the one-class assumption that the training set contains only defect-free samples and confounds the interpretation of the AUROC gains. To obtain a measurement free of this confound, we designed a separate supervised detection experiment (Exp4v2) in which defects are treated as explicitly labeled foreground objects and the synthetic data is used as a conventional labeled training set rather than as a perturbation of a nominal manifold.

Exp4v2 employs YOLOv8 detectors under a baseline-then-augment protocol: a model is first trained on the real training partition (Baseline), and each augmented condition adds synthetic defect images—with their co-saved ROI masks converted to bounding-box labels—to the same training partition at a fixed synth:real budget. Ground-truth masks of the held-out real test split are converted to YOLO-format boxes for evaluation; detection quality is reported as mAP@0.5 over 3 seeds (seeds 1, 2, 42) with paired testing. #5. Discussion
The principal contribution of AROMA is methodological: a Meta Policy Generator that selects ROI modeling and placement policies automatically from measurable dataset complexity statistics (MCI, CCI), removing the per-domain handcrafted compatibility matrix and morphological rules that its predecessor CASDA required. This design advance holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces (metal, organic, textile, machined ceramic) without per-dataset re-engineering. The Meta Policy Generator's Distribution Diagnostics and Candidate Pruning components are described as a design mechanism for making the policy decision reproducible from statistics; we do not attach validated ablation figures (e.g., an MCI/CCI weighting-robustness correlation) to these components in this manuscript, as no such ablation is backed by a committed run, and any such claim is deferred until measured.

Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally. Full-image distribution metrics (FID, KID, LPIPS) are expected to show only small AROMA-vs-Random differences because both pipelines composite identical elastically warped defect pixels and differ only in placement context; we therefore treat supervised detection (§4.4) as the primary evidence and report no numeric FID delta. Under that supervised protocol, **AROMA and random ROI placement are statistically indistinguishable in the dominant case**: across the four canonical datasets of Table 12 (Severstal, MVTec Leather, AITeX, MTD), the AROMA–Random difference lies within seed-to-seed variation on every dataset except one. A measurable benefit appears only on the tiled, single-class AITeX configuration (n=1), where valid measurement, ample baseline headroom, and feasible context-matched placement co-occur; near-ceiling datasets (MTD ≈0.92, MVTec Leather ≈0.81) saturate under random augmentation and leave no headroom for selection to exploit.

We also contribute a mechanistic negative result that explains this pattern at the signal level. The deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting that motivated much of this line of work is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis: the highest-deficit (morphology, context) combinations are precisely those with no real source patch to copy, so deficit is orthogonal to source availability (§3.2.6). Consistent with this, the single AITeX positive occurred with zero deficit among its selected ROIs and was instead driven by contextual compatibility and per-candidate realism; the dataset with the largest selected deficit showed no benefit. The intended value of deficit-aware targeting — filling rare combinations with sources that do not otherwise exist — could therefore only be realized by coupling data-driven placement with a generative engine such as ControlNet, whose ability to actually fill deficit combinations is not yet verified (the elongated aspect ratios of AITeX defects pose a known difficulty).

**Limitations.** Several limitations bound the interpretation of these results and are stated explicitly to forestall over-generalization. First, the sole downstream benefit is a single-dataset observation (n=1, tiled AITeX, Δ ≈ +0.097 mAP@0.5, paired t ≈ 4.51 over 3 seeds); a single positive cannot support a general claim. Second, the deficit-aware mechanism does not drive downstream gains and is refuted as a source of benefit under copy-paste. Third, near-ceiling datasets saturate under random augmentation, so the benefit is confined to a dataset with substantial baseline headroom. Fourth, the AITeX positive is confounded between selection strategy and candidate pool; a symmetric-filter control (applying the same compatibility/quality top-filter to the random arm) is required before attributing the gain to selection, and the result may not transfer beyond the specific tiled, single-class configuration. Fifth, placement is only physically feasible on a subset of datasets: measured clean-background coverage of in-matrix context cells is highly uneven (leather 4.7%, MTD 67.2%, AITeX 77.8%), so context-matched placement is realizable primarily on AITeX, and for full-frame datasets such as Severstal clean-background gating is not meaningful and placement claims are withheld pending separate verification. Finally, all downstream results reported here use copy-paste synthesis over 3 seeds (mean ± standard deviation, paired testing); the ControlNet-based generative arm is in progress and unverified.

#6. Conclusion
We presented AROMA, a framework that automatically selects ROI modeling and placement policies from dataset complexity statistics (MCI and CCI) via a Meta Policy Generator, removing the per-domain handcrafted re-engineering required by prior context-aware synthesis such as CASDA. The principal contribution is this methodological framework for automatic, per-dataset policy selection, which holds independently of downstream accuracy and was applied across heterogeneous industrial surfaces without per-dataset re-engineering. Our empirical findings are reported honestly and conditionally rather than as a uniform performance improvement. In multi-domain downstream detection, AROMA and random ROI placement are statistically indistinguishable in the dominant case; a measurable benefit was observed only on a single dataset (tiled AITeX, n=1) that combined valid measurement, ample baseline headroom, and feasible context-matched placement, while near-ceiling datasets converged with random augmentation. We further contribute a mechanistic negative result: deficit-aware, rare-combination targeting is structurally inert under copy-paste synthesis because high-deficit combinations lack any real source patch to copy (deficit ⟂ source availability). Rather than a demonstration that AROMA improves detection, this work therefore delimits when and why ROI selection helps or fails for patch-based synthesis, and motivates coupling data-driven placement with a generative engine as the direction in which rare-combination targeting could become effective. Future work should reconcile the evaluation roster, run the symmetric-filter control to separate selection from pool effects, and complete the generative arm.

They are available for all four canonical datasets (Severstal, MVTec Leather, AITeX, MTD) and may be included as supplementary figures.
